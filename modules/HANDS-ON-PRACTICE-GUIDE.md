## Section 1: The Philosophy of Hands-On Learning in AI Training {#section-1}

### Why Watching Demos Is Not Enough

There is a version of this curriculum that runs as a presentation. Teacher Scott talks, demos run on the projected screen, slides advance, participants listen. That version fails.

It fails because watching someone use AI fluently is interesting but not transferable. A participant can watch a prior authorization appeal letter materialize on screen in forty seconds and think *that's impressive* — but leave the room with no idea how to replicate it. The distance between observing a tool and using a tool is enormous. The only way across that distance is practice.

This is not unique to AI. Nobody learns to drive by watching someone else drive. Nobody becomes a skilled interviewer by watching their manager conduct interviews. Skills are acquired through doing — through the discomfort of a first awkward attempt, through the surprise of an output that doesn't match expectations, through the second prompt that's better than the first. Hands-on practice creates that cycle. Observation does not.

Every module in this curriculum builds in hands-on time for this reason. Even Module 1 — which is conceptual by design — includes live demos that the facilitator narrates in a way that invites reaction. Module 2 is built entirely around twelve tasks participants complete on their own devices. The hands-on piece is not a supplement to the curriculum. It is the curriculum.

### The "I Made This" Moment

There is a specific moment in every well-delivered session when a participant looks at something on their screen and says — out loud or internally — *I made this.* Not "Teacher Scott made this." Not "the demo showed this." *I* typed a prompt, and this came out of it.

That moment is the single most important thing that happens in a session. It is the moment when the tool stops being abstract and becomes a real capability the participant owns. Participants who experience this moment during the session are dramatically more likely to continue using AI in the days and weeks after the training ends. Participants who observe without producing almost never carry the skill forward.

Your job is to engineer that moment for every participant in the room. Sometimes it happens during Task 1. Sometimes it happens during Task 9 when they use their own real document and the output is startlingly accurate. Sometimes it happens in the Personal Playbook when they paste in their actual situation and see it reflected back with structure and clarity. The task is different. The moment is the same.

Design every hands-on block with that moment in mind. The activity should be specific enough to produce a real output, real enough to feel relevant, and short enough to complete during the session. Longer is not better. The goal is the first success, not the comprehensive mastery.

### The Trust Curve

Most adults walk into an AI training session somewhere on a spectrum between skeptical and intimidated. The skeptics have heard the hype and didn't believe it. The intimidated ones feel like they're late to something everyone else already knows, or that the tools are complicated in a way they'll never get.

Here is the thing that does not move people past either of those positions: explanation. You cannot talk someone out of skepticism or intimidation. You can show them out of it. More specifically: you can hand them the tool and let their own output convince them.

The trust curve in every session looks like this:

1. **Before the first task:** Skepticism or anxiety. People are politely engaged but not convinced.
2. **First task — first output:** Surprise. Even a modest output from a weak prompt creates a reaction.
3. **First iteration:** Shift. When participants see that a follow-up prompt improves the output, they begin to feel agency. This is not a black box. I can direct this.
4. **Third or fourth task:** Ownership. The person who was crossing their arms in the first fifteen minutes is now leaning into the screen.

Explanation can prime this curve. Demos can accelerate it. But only personal practice locks it in. The session that runs forty minutes of content before the first participant task will lose a meaningful percentage of the room. The session that gets participants into their first task within the first thirty minutes has a different energy in hour two.

Plan accordingly. In M1, the live demos serve as the hands-on proxy — the facilitator is handling the tool while participants observe and react. In M2, participants are producing output from the first task block. In M3, they're inserting their own real operational data into prompts within the first fifteen minutes of each track. In the Personal Playbook, account setup happens in the first fifteen minutes so no one spends the rest of the session as a spectator.

### Real Context vs. Fictional Scenarios: When to Use Each

One of the most important facilitation decisions in any session is whether participants work with real context or fictional scenarios. Both have a place. Using the wrong one at the wrong time undermines the learning.

**Use real context when:**
- The participant is the expert on their own situation (always true in M2 and the Personal Playbook)
- The task involves a process or document the participant actually owns
- The goal is producing something the participant will use after the session
- Real data would not constitute PHI or identifiable employee information

The Personal Playbook is built entirely on real context. "Don't use a fake example. Use your real week. Your real resume. Your actual situation." This instruction appears repeatedly in that module's guide because the entire point of the Personal Playbook is building an AI habit grounded in personal relevance. Fictional scenarios are training wheels. Real scenarios are the actual ride.

**Use fictional or de-identified scenarios when:**
- The task involves patient data, client records, or any information that could constitute PHI
- Participants are in a role where using real data would be a compliance risk
- The room is a mixed audience and individual context varies too much for a shared example
- You're in an enterprise healthcare setting and the data belongs to the organization, not the individual

In M3, the track use cases are almost always demonstrated with realistic fictional scenarios — aggregate operational metrics (no-show rates, wait times, budget figures) are safe and real. Patient scenarios are fictional but realistic: a 58-year-old patient with Type 2 diabetes, a 120-bed skilled nursing facility, a Level II Trauma Center. The specificity makes them feel real without creating PHI risk. That's the design principle: fictional details, realistic context.

M2 sits in the middle. For most tasks — email drafting, job descriptions, meeting notes, proposal outlines — participants use their own real work context. Task 11 (the hallucination fact-check) uses pre-generated AI content with planted errors, which is inherently de-identified. The only caution is Task 12 (the capstone), where participants building full workflow outputs should be reminded not to enter client-specific data from actual current proposals.

### AI Drafts, You Decide

This phrase is the governing mindset for all hands-on work across the entire curriculum.

Say it early in every session. Say it again when you run the first demo. Return to it during the debrief. It does three things:

1. **Removes the anxiety of the skeptic.** If the participant is worried that AI will produce something harmful or embarrassing, "AI drafts, you decide" reminds them that they review before they act. The tool doesn't act. They do.
2. **Calibrates the perfectionist.** Some participants want the AI output to be perfect before they feel like the tool worked. "AI drafts, you decide" reframes the goal: the output is a starting point, not a finished product. The professional judgment that used to go into the first draft now goes into reviewing and refining the draft.
3. **Maintains professional accountability.** Especially in healthcare and compliance contexts, participants need to hear explicitly that AI output requires human review. This is not a caveat. It's the workflow. The physician reviews the appeal letter. The compliance officer reviews the policy draft. The HR director reviews the interview guide. AI accelerates the creation. The professional owns the result.

Post this phrase somewhere visible in every room if you can. Write it on the whiteboard. Put it on a slide. It should be the last thing participants hear before they start any task, and the first frame for every debrief.

## Section 2: The Three Delivery Formats {#section-2}

Every hands-on moment in every module maps to one of three delivery formats. The right format depends on the audience, the module, the room setup, and the technology environment. Understanding when to use each — and how to execute each well — is the most important facilitation skill in this curriculum.

### Format 1: Observe + Discuss

**How it works:**

Teacher Scott runs the AI tool live on the projected screen. Participants watch the full prompt-to-output cycle, react, and discuss. No devices required from participants. The facilitator narrates the experience — not just showing what appeared, but commenting on the input, explaining the structural choices in the prompt, reading the output out loud, and asking the room questions before explaining what to notice.

This is not passive. The facilitator is active; the participants are verbally engaged. The difference from lecture is that the content being discussed is live output generated in real time, not pre-prepared slides.

**When to choose it:**

- Module 1 throughout — conceptual foundation sessions where the goal is building mental models, not personal tool use
- Module 4 with executive audiences — C-suite and board-level rooms where asking executives to type prompts on a laptop would create friction that overshadows the content
- Any setting where personal devices are unavailable or where corporate IT has blocked access to all AI tools (pivot to this format rather than canceling the hands-on component)
- Opening demos in any module before participants have warmed up to the tool
- When a concept needs to land visually before participants attempt it themselves

**Pros:**
- No technology requirements for participants — works in any room
- Facilitator controls the pace and quality of the output
- Catches confusion before it multiplies across thirty individual screens
- Best format for executive audiences who respond to seeing it done well rather than doing it themselves

**Cons:**
- Participants do not produce their own output — the "I made this" moment does not happen
- Some participants mentally disengage during the watch phase, especially those who are already comfortable with the tools
- Learning retention is lower than Model → Replicate or Guided Independent Practice
- If the demo goes wrong, there is no fallback — the facilitator is on camera, so to speak

**Setup requirements:**
- Reliable internet connection and working projection
- AI tool pre-loaded and logged in on the facilitator's device
- All demo prompts pre-typed in a scratch document (never type live — see Section 5)
- No advance participant prep required

**Lead time:** Minimal. This format requires only facilitator preparation, not participant prep or IT coordination.

**What Teacher Scott does differently in this format:**

The facilitator's job in Format 1 is to make the output visible and interpretable for the room, not just to run the demo. This requires specific narration skills:

- **Narrate the input before you paste.** "Here's what I'm asking for, and here's why I built it this way. Notice the role I gave it. Notice the context. Notice what I'm asking it NOT to do." This turns a demo into a lesson in prompt construction.
- **Read the output out loud.** Do not let participants read at their own pace in silence. The group experience of reading together creates a shared reaction that a silent room cannot. Read the key sections. Pause at the good parts.
- **Point before you explain.** "Notice this section. See what it did with the format instruction." Let participants see it before you explain what they're seeing. This keeps the observation theirs, not yours.
- **Ask before you analyze.** "What do you notice?" should precede any facilitator analysis. The room's reaction is more valuable than your commentary, and eliciting it first makes participants feel seen and heard.
- **Iterate at least once.** Every Format 1 demo must include a follow-up prompt. The single most important concept in the curriculum is that AI is a conversation, not a vending machine. A demo that shows only the first output teaches nothing about the actual workflow.

### Format 2: Model → Replicate

**How it works:**

Teacher Scott runs the task live on the projected screen — the full four-step cycle: Model it → Explain it → Participants try → Debrief. Participants then replicate the task on their own devices, adapting the starter prompt to their own context. The facilitator circulates during participant work time.

This is the standard hands-on format for Module 2 Tasks 1–5 and for the first demo in each Module 3 track.

**The four-step cycle in detail:**

**Step 1 — Model it.** Run the prompt yourself, projected on screen. Narrate as you go: "Here's what I'm asking for and why I built the prompt this way." Point to each element (Role, Task, Context, Format, Constraints) as you paste. Read the key parts of the output out loud. Then iterate once — show at least one follow-up prompt before moving to Step 2.

**Step 2 — Explain it.** After the first output lands, pause. Ask: "What do you notice?" Let two or three people react before you explain anything. Then connect their observations to the framework: "That's why we included the format instruction" or "Notice what it missed — that's because we left out the constraint." Keep this to ninety seconds. You're not re-teaching; you're connecting the demo to the principle.

**Step 3 — Participants try.** "Your turn. Use the starter prompt on screen, adjust it for your context, and run it." Give a clear time limit: three to five minutes for most tasks. Circulate. Coach individuals briefly — one targeted question per person, not a full re-explanation. Let people struggle slightly. First outputs are supposed to be imperfect. That's the teaching moment.

**Step 4 — Debrief.** "Who got something useful? Who got garbage? What made the difference?" Take two or three real examples from the room. If someone got a weak output, diagnose it live: "What context did you leave out? What format instruction would have helped?" This is the most valuable five minutes of each task cycle. Do not skip it.

**When to choose it:**

- Module 2, Tasks 1–5 (the guided section)
- First demo in each Module 3 track before releasing participants to independent practice
- Any task where participants are tool-naive or where the concept needs to be seen before it can be attempted
- Groups where individual practice pace varies significantly — the model phase provides a concrete anchor

**Pros:**
- Participants see the full cycle before attempting it — reduces confusion and wasted practice time
- Facilitator controls the pacing of the demo
- The debrief after participant practice produces the richest learning moments in the session
- Participants leave with real output they produced themselves

**Cons:**
- Watch time reduces hands-on time — demos should not exceed four minutes or participants get restless
- Some participants will mentally check out during the watch phase if they've already run ahead
- Requires working devices and tool access for all participants

**Setup requirements:**
- Facilitator device: AI tool pre-loaded, demo prompts pre-typed in scratch doc
- Participant devices: accounts set up (ideally before the session via pre-session email), internet access confirmed
- Task instructions on a projected slide or handout so participants know exactly what to try

**Lead time:** Participant prep email should go out 48–72 hours before the session asking participants to create free ChatGPT and Perplexity accounts. Account setup during the session is possible but costs 5–7 minutes.

**Managing the pace gap — the most common Format 2 challenge:**

In any group of fifteen or more participants, some will finish the task in sixty seconds and some will need six minutes. If you don't have a plan for fast finishers, they will either go off-topic (fine, if it's AI-related) or disengage (not fine).

**Pre-type, never cold-type — the non-negotiable rule for Format 2 (and all formats):**

> **Never type a demo prompt from scratch in front of participants.** The demo is about showing the output and iteration, not about showing yourself type. Watching a facilitator type a long prompt is boring, error-prone, and wastes time. Have every demo prompt pre-typed in a plain-text scratch document. Copy, paste, run. This applies to every demo in every module.

Have follow-up challenges ready for every task — printed, on a slide, or written on the board. "If you've finished, try this: [follow-up challenge]." These challenges should extend the task, not start a new one. For Task 1 (the email), the follow-up is "ask it to write three versions — formal, conversational, and urgent." For Task 3 (the job description), it's "rewrite this for a travel position instead of a permanent hire." Fast finishers stay in the task; slow finishers aren't left behind.

### Format 3: Guided Independent Practice

**How it works:**

Participants work through tasks independently or in pairs/table groups, using a prompt provided on screen or in a handout. Teacher Scott circulates the room, coaches individuals who are stuck, observes outputs, and facilitates the debrief. There is no group demo before the task — participants apply what they've already learned in the session.

This is the format for Module 2 Tasks 6–12, for Module 3 use cases after the first demo in each track, for Module 4's table group capstone, and for all hands-on moments in the Personal Playbook.

**When to choose it:**

- After participants have demonstrated they can run the basic cycle independently (typically after Task 5 in M2)
- Module 3 use cases after the track has been introduced — participants should have enough fluency to adapt prompts without watching a full demo first
- Module 4 capstone — table groups working through the 90-Day Blueprint
- Personal Playbook — all use cases, since participants are working from their own personal context rather than a shared scenario

**Pros:**
- Maximum hands-on time — no watch time
- Produces the most diverse and interesting outputs, which makes the debrief richer
- Builds actual independence — participants are practicing the skill they'll use on Monday
- In Personal Playbook, real-context practice creates the strongest habit-formation

**Cons:**
- Requires more facilitation skill — the facilitator cannot rely on the demo to carry the room
- Pace variation is wider — fast finishers need challenges, slow starters need coaching
- Output quality is more variable — some participants will struggle without a model to follow
- Participants who are stuck can fall significantly behind if not caught early

**Setup requirements:**
- All task prompts visible on screen or in a handout — participants need to know exactly what to do
- Facilitator is circulating, not presenting — keep moving, keep coaching
- Timer visible to the facilitator (not necessarily to participants)

**Lead time:** Same as Format 2 for participant prep. For enterprise clients, tool access should be confirmed 1–2 weeks in advance.

**The Personal Playbook variant:**

In the Personal Playbook, Format 3 has a specific instruction that overrides any generic guidance: participants use their own real-life situations, not provided scenarios. "Don't use a fake example. Use your real week. Your real resume. Your actual situation. The prompt will be twice as useful when the context is real." This instruction is the core of why the Personal Playbook works. Enforce it gently but consistently.

**The debrief as the core skill in Format 3:**

In Formats 1 and 2, the demo anchors the debrief. In Format 3, the debrief has to stand on its own. This is where the most important facilitation skill in the curriculum lives — see Section 6 for the full debrief protocol.

## Section 3: Tool Access — The Three Scenarios {#section-3}

Before every session, know which tool access scenario you're walking into. Each requires a different setup approach and a different set of expectations.

### Scenario A: Open Access (Participants Have or Create Their Own Accounts)

**What it looks like:**
Participants bring their own devices — laptops, tablets, or phones — and use personal accounts they either already have or create at the start of the session. This is the standard setup for open-enrollment sessions, community workshops, most M2 deliveries, and the Personal Playbook.

**Why this is the ideal scenario:**
Participants leave with an account they own, a history of their session outputs, and a tool they can access immediately on Monday morning. There is no intermediary between the participant and the capability. This is the setup that most reliably produces the "I made this" moment — because it was made in their account, on their device, with their login.

Recommend this setup whenever you can. Make it explicit in the pre-session communication: "Bring your phone or laptop — we're going to actually use AI today, not just talk about it."

**Tool setup walkthrough — getting a group set up in under 5 minutes:**

Run this as a structured 5-minute block. Don't leave it to chance or assume everyone arrived ready. Do it on screen, step by step.

1. **Pick the tool:** Recommend ChatGPT for M2 and M4. Recommend ChatGPT + Perplexity for M3 and the Personal Playbook.
2. **Open the browser.** Go to `chatgpt.com`.
3. **Click Sign Up.** Use an existing Google account or email. The signup takes about 60 seconds.
4. **Skip any paid upgrade prompts.** Free tier is sufficient for everything in this curriculum.
5. **Land on the chat screen.** Once they see the white text box, they're ready.
6. **Repeat for Perplexity.** Go to `perplexity.ai`, click Sign Up.

**What to say during setup:**
> "If you already have an account, great — just log in. If you're creating one right now, that's exactly the right thing to be doing. You're two minutes away from a tool that will save you hours every week."

**When someone gets stuck on account creation:** Use the buddy system. Pair them with a neighbor who's already in. This creates peer-to-peer learning in the room and is faster than one facilitator troubleshooting fifteen stragglers. Say it proactively: "If you're done, raise your hand — you're going to help a neighbor who isn't there yet."

**Recommended privacy settings to enable:** Before the first task, walk participants through these settings. It takes sixty seconds per tool and builds trust.

- **ChatGPT:** Settings → Data Controls → "Improve the model for everyone" → Off. This disables your conversation history from being used in model training.
- **Perplexity:** Settings → Privacy → turn off "Use my data to improve Perplexity." Similar effect.

Mention this out loud: "I want to show you one thing before we start." Run through the setting on your demo device. Then: "This takes thirty seconds and it's worth it, especially if you're going to enter any real work context into the session."

**Free tier limitations to know and manage:**

- **Rate limits:** On ChatGPT free tier, heavy concurrent use (fifteen or more participants all running long prompts at the same time) can trigger slowdowns. This is manageable — it's a teaching moment about tool differences, not a failure. Say: "Some of you may see a slower response — that's a rate limit on the free tier. Take the extra 10 seconds to read your neighbor's prompt while you wait."
- **Context window:** ChatGPT free tier has a smaller context window than paid tiers. For most M2 tasks this is irrelevant. For long-document tasks (Task 2 summarization, Task 9 SOP building), recommend Claude's free tier, which handles longer inputs better.
- **GPT-4o availability:** Free tier users can access GPT-4o but with a daily message limit. For most sessions this is sufficient. If a participant hits the limit, have them switch to Claude or Perplexity as an alternative.

### Scenario B: Corporate IT Restrictions (Managed Devices, Approved Tools Only)

**What it looks like:**
The client's participants are on managed corporate devices. Personal account sign-in may be blocked by IT policy. Only specific tools — often Microsoft Copilot, a provisioned ChatGPT Team workspace, or a Perplexity Space — are accessible.

**Reality check:** Most enterprise healthcare clients have some version of this. Do not be surprised by it. Identify the scenario during your pre-session client call, not on the morning of delivery.

**Pre-session client checklist — 5 questions to answer before walking in:**

Run through these with your client contact during the pre-session call (aim for 48–72 hours before the session at minimum):

1. **What AI tools are currently approved for use on corporate devices?** Get a specific list, not a vague answer. "We use Microsoft" is not enough. Does that mean Copilot is enabled in M365? Is it available to all staff?
2. **Are personal accounts permitted on corporate devices during a training session?** Some clients will allow this as a training exception. Others will not. Know before you walk in.
3. **Is there a BAA-covered enterprise tier available?** For healthcare clients, this is the distinction that matters most. ChatGPT Enterprise and Microsoft Copilot M365 can have BAAs in place. The free consumer tiers cannot. This determines what data can be used in hands-on tasks.
4. **Who is the IT or compliance contact I can reach on the day of the session if access breaks?** Get a name and direct phone number. Not a helpdesk ticket system. A person.
5. **Has a Perplexity Space, ChatGPT Team workspace, or other shared access environment been provisioned?** If the client wants to do hands-on work in an approved environment, someone needs to set it up in advance. That setup takes 30–60 minutes and requires IT involvement. This is not a day-of task.

**Tool mapping — adapting module tasks to Microsoft Copilot:**

If only Microsoft Copilot is approved, here's how to handle each module's key tasks. Copilot covers most of what this curriculum needs. The gaps are in real-time research and specific tool-switching exercises.

| Task / Use Case | Copilot Adaptation |
|---|---|
| M2 Task 1 — Email drafting | Fully supported in Copilot (Outlook integration is ideal) |
| M2 Task 2 — Document summarization | Fully supported; Copilot in Word handles this natively |
| M2 Task 3 — Job description | Fully supported in Copilot Chat |
| M2 Task 4 — Meeting notes | Fully supported; Copilot in Teams handles transcription and summary |
| M2 Task 5 — Policy plain language | Fully supported |
| M2 Task 8 — Research brief (Perplexity) | Approximate in Copilot Chat with Bing Search enabled; note citations will differ |
| M2 Tasks 6, 7, 9, 10, 12 | Fully supported in Copilot Chat |
| M2 Task 11 — Hallucination fact-check | Fully supported; use pre-generated paragraph with planted errors |
| M3 Track A — Healthcare ops prompts | Fully supported; use de-identified scenarios |
| M3 Track B — Staffing / HR prompts | Fully supported |
| M3 Track C — Compliance prompts | Fully supported; Copilot Enterprise tier preferred for PHI-adjacent tasks |
| M3 Track D — Leadership / governance | Fully supported |
| M4 Governance and business case prompts | Fully supported |
| Personal Playbook — all use cases | Supported; recommend using personal accounts in non-corporate sessions |

**If no tool is approved:**

Do not force hands-on work with no tool access. Pivot to Format 1 (Observe + Discuss) for that client. Run every demo yourself. Debrief deeply. Use the extra time for discussion rather than stretching thin hands-on moments that produce nothing. A well-run Observe + Discuss session is better than a broken hands-on session.

Document the gap clearly in your post-session notes. Flag it to the client: "For the next session, I'd recommend 2 weeks advance coordination with IT to provision tool access. Here's what that looks like."

**Recommended minimum lead time for enterprise clients:** 2 weeks for tool access to be provisioned, tested, and confirmed. Do not accept "we'll figure it out the week of" from a client contact. That phrasing means it will not be ready.

### Scenario C: Mixed Room (Some Participants Have Access, Some Don't)

**What it looks like:**
Common in open-enrollment sessions, community workshops, multi-organization trainings, and any setting where participants come from different companies or contexts. Some participants arrive with ChatGPT accounts already set up. Some have never opened the tool. Some are on corporate devices with restrictions; others are on personal devices.

**How to manage it:**

- **Pair participants.** Deliberately pair someone with access and an account with someone who doesn't have either. This creates the buddy system organically and means no one is left behind in the first task while someone else is still creating an account.
- **Project one shared screen at each table.** If tables have a mix of access levels, ask one participant per table to volunteer their screen to project via a cable or shared display. The table works off that screen while stragglers get set up.
- **Use the buddy system during account setup.** "If you're already logged in, raise your hand. You're helping a neighbor get set up right now." This is faster than the facilitator troubleshooting individually and creates a positive social dynamic.
- **Identify the no-account contingency early.** At the start of the session, before the first task, identify who does not have access. Give those participants the explicit pairing assignment. Don't leave it to chance.

### Pre-Session Technology Checklist

Run this checklist before every session, regardless of format or client type. It takes five minutes and prevents the most common session-day failures.

**24–48 hours before:**
- [ ] Pre-session participant email sent (if applicable) with account creation instructions and any documents to bring
- [ ] If enterprise client: IT access confirmed with a specific person, not just assumed
- [ ] Demo prompts written and saved in a plain-text scratch document — not in the slide deck, not in your head
- [ ] Know which track(s) you're running for M3; confirm with client contact

**Day of, before participants arrive:**
- [ ] Presentation device: slides loaded and tested; screen mirroring or cable connected and confirmed
- [ ] AI tools open in browser tabs: ChatGPT logged in (your account), Perplexity in a second tab, Claude in a third if needed for M2 Tasks 2, 5, or 9
- [ ] Demo prompts document open and visible — ready to copy and paste
- [ ] Internet connection tested with an actual tool query, not just a page load

- [ ] If enterprise: shared access link or provisioned workspace confirmed live
- [ ] Task instruction slides or handout visible from your presentation view
- [ ] Timer available (phone, laptop corner clock, or projected timer app)
- [ ] Whiteboard, flip chart, or sticky notes for opening questions and debrief capture

**At session start:**
- [ ] WiFi network name and password written on the board or slide (participants will ask)
- [ ] Account setup block executed (5 minutes; do not skip)

## Section 4: The PHI / Data Privacy Protocol {#section-4}

> **This protocol applies to all business modules (M1–M4).** It does NOT apply in the same way to the Personal Playbook, where the concern is personal data privacy rather than HIPAA-covered protected health information. See Section 9 for Personal Playbook-specific privacy guidance.

### The Mandatory Verbal Disclosure

Read this disclosure aloud — verbatim or in your own words — at the start of every business module hands-on section. Do not skip it. Do not summarize it to one sentence. In healthcare settings with clinical or compliance staff present, this disclosure is your credibility.

```
Before we do any hands-on work today, I need to say something clearly:
do not enter real patient information into any AI tool during this session.

That means no real patient names, no medical record numbers, no dates of birth,
no diagnosis codes tied to a real person, no claim data — nothing that would
constitute PHI under HIPAA.

If we're building an example with your real data, use de-identified or realistic
fictional numbers. Use your actual no-show rate — that's an aggregate operational
metric, not PHI. Use your actual procedure volume, your actual supply costs.
But if the example involves a patient scenario, we create a fictional patient together.

The reason I'm saying this out loud is that the examples in this module will feel
very real and very specific — and when something feels real, it's easy to just paste
in what you have. Don't. Make it fictional. Realistic, but fictional.

This is exactly the same data classification discipline you'd apply to email.
You wouldn't send a patient's chart in a personal Gmail. Same logic applies here.
```

### The Three Data Tiers

Use this framework when explaining to participants what they can and cannot use. It maps directly to the security tier framework in Module 4 and should feel familiar to participants who've completed that module.

**Tier 1 — Aggregate operational metrics: Safe for consumer AI tools**

Examples: no-show rate (18%), ED wait time average (47 minutes), staff overtime percentage (12%), monthly supply cost totals ($1.2M). These are operational performance metrics. They describe the system, not any individual. They are not PHI. Participants can and should use real numbers here — it makes the demos more powerful and the outputs more relevant.

**Tier 2 — De-identified or fictional scenarios: Safe for consumer AI tools**

Examples: a fictional patient with Type 2 diabetes and peripheral neuropathy (no name, no MRN, no DOB), a fictional 120-bed skilled nursing facility, a fictional vendor with a 71% on-time delivery rate. These are realistic enough to produce useful output but do not contain PHI. This is the design principle behind all of the clinical scenarios in Module 3.

The "realistic fictional" technique: Coach participants to use their real operational numbers while fictionalizing any patient or individual details. "Use your actual no-show rate. Use your actual day of the week when no-shows spike. But the patient in the reminder sequence — she's fictional. Her name, her condition, her contact information are all made up." Participants can combine these: real operational context + fictional individual details = safe and useful.

**Tier 3 — PHI or identified patient data: Never in consumer tools; only in HIPAA BAA-covered enterprise tiers**

Examples: real patient names, real medical record numbers, real diagnoses tied to an identified person, claim data with patient identifiers. This category should never enter ChatGPT free tier, Claude free tier, or any consumer tool without a BAA in place. In enterprise tiers with a BAA (ChatGPT Enterprise, Microsoft Copilot M365 with proper configuration), PHI is permitted based on the organization's governance policy.

### What to Do If a Participant Enters PHI Accidentally

This happens. Stay calm. It is not a crisis. Here is the response:

1. **Notice it quietly if you can.** If you're circulating and you see PHI on a participant's screen, approach them individually rather than calling it out to the room.
2. **Stop the input, don't undo the session.** Say: "Hey — I want to flag something. That looks like it might have real patient identifiers in it. Let's not enter anything else in this thread. Can you open a fresh chat?"
3. **Don't make it a crisis.** The participant is embarrassed. The goal is not to amplify that. The goal is to redirect without shutting down their engagement with the session.
4. **Explain why calmly.** "Consumer tools don't have a BAA. The same reason you wouldn't email this from a personal account — same rule applies here. In your organization's enterprise tier, this conversation would look different. But for today's session, let's use fictional details."
5. **Move on.** Don't reference it again in the debrief. Don't use it as a room-wide teaching moment (the participant will feel exposed). If you need to make the point to the room, do it generally at a natural transition point without identifying the individual.

## Section 5: The Live Demo Protocol {#section-5}

Every live demo in every module follows this master protocol. Deviations from this protocol are the most common cause of demos that underperform.

### The Seven Rules

**Rule 1: Pre-type everything.**

Never cold-type a demo prompt in front of participants. Have every prompt pre-written in a plain-text scratch document before the session starts. During the demo, copy, paste, run. The demo is about showing the output and the iteration — not about watching the facilitator type. Typing live introduces errors, wastes time, and undercuts the facilitator's credibility. This rule has no exceptions.

> The scratch document should be open on your device at all times. It should contain every demo prompt for the session in order, clearly labeled by task or slide number. When you're ready to run a demo, click into the document, select the prompt, copy it, switch to the AI tool tab, paste, hit enter.

**Rule 2: Narrate the input before you paste.**

Before you paste the prompt, tell the room what you're about to give the AI and why you built the prompt the way you did. "Here's what I'm asking, and here's why I built it this way. Notice the role I gave it. Notice the context. Notice what I explicitly told it NOT to do." This narration turns a demo into a lesson. Without it, participants see the output but don't understand why the input produced it.

**Rule 3: Read the output out loud.**

Don't let participants read silently at their own pace. The group experience of reading together creates a shared reaction. Read the key sections of the output. Pause at the good parts. Slow down when something surprising appears. Reading out loud also keeps you in control of pacing — you decide what the room focuses on.

**Rule 4: Point before you explain.**

"Notice this part. See how it did X." Point to specific sections of the output and let participants see what you're pointing to before you explain it. This keeps the observation theirs before you claim it. If you explain before they've had a chance to see it, the learning belongs to you. If you point first and ask "what do you notice about this section?", the learning belongs to them.

**Rule 5: Iterate once.**

Every demo must include at least one follow-up prompt. This is the single most important teaching moment in any demo. Showing only the first output teaches participants that AI is a one-shot tool — you ask, it answers, done. Showing a follow-up prompt teaches them that AI is a conversation — and that the first output is almost never the last one. Write on the whiteboard if you need to: *AI is a conversation, not a vending machine.* Then run the follow-up live.

**Rule 6: Ask before you debrief.**

After the output appears, say: "What do you notice?" — not "here's what you should notice." Let the room react first. Wait through the silence if necessary. The room's first observations are more valuable than your commentary, because they reveal what landed and what didn't. Use the room's observations as your debrief material; don't prepare the debrief before you know what they saw.

**Rule 7: Four-minute maximum.**

No demo runs longer than four minutes from first keystroke to debrief question. If a demo is running long, cut to the output — jump to the end result and debrief what's there rather than narrating every step of generation. Long demos lose the room. The best demos feel efficient: here's the input, here's what came out, here's the follow-up, what do you notice? That cycle should not take more than four minutes.

### Demo Troubleshooting Table

| What goes wrong | What to do |
|---|---|
| **Slow response / tool is loading** | Narrate while you wait: "While this generates — notice what I included in the prompt structure. I gave it a Role, a specific Task, the Context it needs, and a Format instruction. It's going to take about fifteen seconds. That's fine." Never stare silently at a loading screen. |
| **Unexpected output** — the AI produced something different than planned | "Interesting — this isn't quite what I expected. Let's look at why. What context did we leave out? What constraint would change this?" Turn the unexpected output into a live prompt-improvement exercise. This is actually a better teaching moment than a perfect output. |
| **Participant says "mine gave a different answer"** | "Perfect. That's one of the most important things to know about AI. Let's look at yours next to mine. What's different about them? What in the prompt caused the difference?" The most valuable teaching moment in the room is two different outputs from two different prompts. Use it. |
| **Tool is down** | Don't panic. Switch to a second tool tab (you have Claude and Perplexity open, right?). If all tools are down, run Format 1 with a pre-generated output you saved from your prep session. Keep a screenshot or text file of key demo outputs in your prep materials as insurance. |
| **Participant asks a question you can't answer live** | "That's a great question — I want to give you a real answer on that, not a quick one. Let me park it and come back to it in the debrief. Or if it's specific to your org, let's connect after." Never make up an answer to an AI question you don't know. Credibility is your most important asset. "I don't know, but let's find out" is fine. |
| **Output contains a clear error or hallucination** | Don't skip past it. This is the fact-check lesson made real. "Notice this — this section is wrong. Let me show you why." Walk through the error, identify the claim, verify it against a real source. This is the moment Task 11 was designed to illustrate, but it can happen in any demo. Use it. |
| **Participant's output contains PHI** | See Section 4. Handle individually, calmly, without making it a room-wide moment. |
| **WiFi fails** | Switch to personal hotspot (which you have ready — see pre-session checklist). Run Format 1 with pre-generated outputs if hotspot also fails. Acknowledge it: "Technology, right? Let's pivot." |

## Section 6: The Debrief — Making Learning Stick {#section-6}

Most facilitators rush the debrief or skip it entirely. This is the most expensive mistake in an AI training session. The demo produces engagement. The debrief produces learning.

Here is what happens when you skip the debrief: participants produce output, feel a moment of excitement or confusion, and then move to the next task without converting that experience into any durable understanding. They leave the session with the memory of doing tasks but not the insight about why any of it worked.

Here is what happens when you run a good debrief: the room tells you what they noticed, surfaces what surprised them, starts connecting the output quality to the prompt quality, and begins identifying where in their actual work they'd use this. That's the difference between training entertainment and training that changes behavior.

### The Four Debrief Questions

Use one or two per task. Never use all four every time — that turns the debrief into an interrogation. Choose based on what happened during the task.

**Question 1: "What did you notice?"**
(Use for: observation and awareness)

This is your opening question for almost every debrief. It's open enough to surface any observation and doesn't prime participants toward a specific response. Wait through the silence if necessary — the first person to speak will give you something to work with, and others will follow.

**Question 2: "What would you change about the prompt?"**
(Use for: building iteration mindset; best after a weak or mediocre output)

This is the most important question for building the habit of prompt improvement. It reframes a weak output as information rather than failure: "The output wasn't quite right — what would you add or remove from the prompt to fix it?" This teaches prompt debugging, which is the core skill participants need for Monday morning.

**Question 3: "Where in your actual work would you use this?"**
(Use for: application and transfer; best after a strong output that resonated)

This question bridges the session to the real workflow. It's the one that most directly addresses whether any of this will be used after participants leave the room. Don't rush it. Let people think. The specific answers — "I'd use this for the monthly ops report that takes me three hours" — are the ones that will drive post-session behavior.

**Question 4: "What surprised you?"**
(Use for: mindset shift; most powerful at early tasks when participants are still calibrating)

Surprise is the signal that something exceeded or contradicted expectations. When participants are surprised by the quality of an output, that's the moment the trust curve moves. Ask about it. "What surprised you?" is better than "was that better than you expected?" because it doesn't prime the answer and it opens up both positive and negative surprise.

### How to Handle "The Output Was Bad"

This is the most common and most useful debrief moment in any session. When a participant says "mine was garbage" or "I didn't get anything useful," do not move past it.

Instead:
1. "Thank you for sharing that. Can you tell me what you put in the prompt?" Ask them to read it or show it.
2. Diagnose it live: "Which of the five elements was missing? Did you give it a role? Did you give it context? Did you specify a format?" Walk through the framework against their actual prompt.
3. "What would you add?" Let the participant or the room suggest the fix.
4. If time allows: "Try the revised version right now." Two minutes. Then check back.

This sequence does more to teach prompt construction than any demo you'll run. The participant with the bad output — if you handle it well — often becomes the strongest practitioner in the room by the end of the session, because they've actually debugged something. The participant with the perfect first output learned less.

### The "Show Me Yours" Technique

After any task where participants have run their own prompts, ask: "Is anyone willing to share their output with the room? Just read the first paragraph, or put your screen on the projector."

When two or three participants share, something powerful happens: the room can see that different prompts produced different outputs. This visual comparison is the fastest way to teach why specificity matters — not through explanation, but through direct evidence. "Look at these two outputs. Same task. Different prompts. What's different about the outputs, and what in the prompt caused that difference?"

In some rooms this requires a nudge: "I'll go first — here's my output from the demo. Now I want to see one that's different." The facilitator's willingness to share first usually unlocks the room.

### Time Allocation

- **Standard task debrief:** 2–3 minutes. Cap it and move on.
- **Strong reaction or rich divergence in outputs:** Up to 5 minutes. Worth it if the room is generating insight.
- **No reaction, flat debrief:** Don't force it. Acknowledge the result, state the learning principle, move on. "If the output was underwhelming, that's good data — it tells us what the prompt was missing. We'll pick that up in the next task."

Do not let debriefs run past 5 minutes per task. Momentum is a real thing in a hands-on session. Losing it mid-session is hard to recover from.

## Section 7: Managing the Room During Hands-On Practice {#section-7}

### The Pace Gap

In any group of more than eight participants, some will finish the task in sixty seconds and some will need five minutes. This gap is inevitable. Planning for it is not optional.

**For fast finishers:** Have a follow-up challenge ready for every task. It should be:
- Visible — either on a projected slide or in a handout
- Related to the task just completed — not a new task entirely
- Optional, not mandatory — "if you've finished, try this next"

Examples of good follow-up challenges:
- Task 1 (email): "Ask it to write three versions — formal, conversational, and urgent."
- Task 3 (job description): "Rewrite it for a travel position instead of a permanent hire."
- Task 8 (Perplexity research): "Find one number in the output and verify it against a primary source."
- Task 12 (capstone): "Now ask AI where AI could be further integrated into the workflow you just built."

Fast finishers who have a productive next step will not disengage or distract others. Fast finishers who have nothing to do will start surfing or talking.

**For slow starters:** The first five minutes of any independent task reveal who needs help. Circulate within the first two minutes. If you see someone whose screen hasn't changed (cursor blinking in an empty prompt box), approach calmly. Don't hover. Ask: "What are you thinking about starting with?" — not "do you need help?" The former opens a productive conversation. The latter signals distress.

### The Stuck Participant

Signs someone is stuck vs. just slow:
- Screen hasn't changed in more than three minutes
- Body language: leaning back, looking around rather than at screen, arms crossed
- Has not typed anything
- Appears frustrated rather than focused

Signs someone is just slow:
- Screen is changing — they're typing, reading, revising
- Body language is focused — leaning forward, engaged with device
- They look like they're thinking, not frozen

**How to approach a stuck participant without embarrassing them:**

Come from the side or behind, not from the front (which feels like surveillance). Say quietly: "How's it going? What are you working on?" Listen to their answer before offering anything. Often, saying the situation out loud is enough — they'll talk themselves to a prompt.

If they're still stuck: "What's the real-world version of this task that you'd want to do? Tell me in one sentence." When they tell you, say: "That's your prompt — almost word for word. Start with that and we'll refine it." Give them the first word to type and step away. Coming back in two minutes to see what came out is more effective than standing over them while they type.

### The Skeptic Who Won't Try

Don't force it. The skeptic who physically won't engage with the tool during Task 1 is not lost — they're watching. What usually happens is that by Task 3, when they've seen multiple people in the room react positively and when the examples have landed as relevant to their specific work, the skeptic quietly opens their laptop.

What helps:
- Pair them with an engaged participant, framing it as collaboration rather than assistance: "Would you two work through this one together?"
- Acknowledge the skepticism directly, without pressing: "Some of you may be in 'I'll watch and evaluate' mode right now. That's fine. You'll know when something feels worth trying."
- Find the task that's closest to their specific pain. If the skeptic is a recruiter, Task 3 (job description) is probably the unlock. If they're a compliance officer, Tasks 5 and the M3 compliance track will do it. The right use case at the right moment is more persuasive than any amount of encouragement.

### The Off-Topic Rabbit Hole

Participant starts using AI for something completely unrelated to the task — often something personal or something from a completely different role.

First: don't be alarmed. This is often a sign of genuine engagement, not disrespect. They're exploring the tool because they're excited about it.

How to redirect: "I love that you're exploring — that's exactly the right instinct. Let's finish this task as a group and then I'll give you five minutes at the end to try that." Or: "That sounds like it might be a Personal Playbook use case — let's put a pin in it." Don't shut it down; redirect it. The participant who goes off-topic is almost always more engaged than the one who's politely following along.

### The "My Output Was Different" Moment

This is not a problem. This is the most valuable teaching moment in the room.

When a participant says their output was different from the demo or from a neighbor's output, immediately elevate it: "Let's look at both. Put yours up — read the first paragraph." Then compare. "Same task. What's different? What in your prompt caused the difference?"

This moment teaches, in one live example, more about why specificity matters than any amount of explanation. Use it every time it happens. If it doesn't happen naturally in a session, engineer it by asking two participants to compare outputs during the debrief.

### Large Groups (20+)

What changes in groups of twenty or more participants:

**Format selection:** Format 3 (Guided Independent Practice) becomes harder to manage well in a large group. The debrief is harder to facilitate because you can't hear from everyone, and circulating becomes physically difficult. Consider:
- Running Format 2 for more tasks (more demos, less independent practice time)
- Pairing participants for all tasks, which halves the number of individual screens you're managing
- Using table groups for the debrief rather than full-room debrief

**Pacing:** Add 30–60 seconds to each task time allowance. In large groups, account setup takes longer, getting the room's attention back after independent practice takes longer, and debrief takes longer.

**Debrief management:** In a room of twenty or more, open debrief questions ("who wants to share?") can produce either too many volunteers or none. Manage this by pre-identifying two or three participants whose output you noticed during circulation: "I'm going to call on two people I noticed had interesting outputs. [Name], can you read your first line?"

**PHI vigilance:** Larger rooms require more active monitoring during tasks, especially in M3. The probability that at least one participant enters PHI accidentally scales with group size. Circulate continuously. You won't catch everything, but steady movement is better than stationary oversight.

## Section 9: The Personal Playbook Difference {#section-9}

The Personal Playbook is not a simplified version of the business modules. It is a fundamentally different kind of session, requiring a different facilitation posture, different privacy guidance, and a different measure of success. This section covers what makes it distinct and how to deliver it well.

### Account Setup Is Part of the Session

In the business modules, account setup is pre-session overhead — handled via the participant prep email, completed before people walk in the room. In the Personal Playbook, account setup is built into the session itself. It is a formal five-minute block, not an afterthought.

Why: the Personal Playbook frequently serves audiences who are new to AI tools entirely — community settings, general adult learners, participants who don't have a work IT team managing their software or an HR department sending them pre-session materials. For these participants, getting into a tool for the first time is not friction. It is a victory. Treat it that way.

**Run the account setup walkthrough after Slide 3 (Guardrails), before Part 1 begins.** Introduce it like this:

> "Before we get into the actual use cases — we're going to take five minutes to make sure everyone has a tool open and ready. Because I don't want you watching me do this all day. I want you to actually try it."

**Step-by-step to walk the group through:**
1. Pick a tool: ChatGPT (free tier) for most use cases; mention Perplexity for research tasks.
2. Go to `chatgpt.com`.
3. Click "Sign up" — use Google account or email. Takes about 60 seconds.
4. Skip any paid upgrade prompts.
5. Land on the chat screen — once they see the text box, they're ready.

**If someone says "I don't want to create an account":** Respect it. Tell them to watch along and try it at home. The prompts will be in their follow-up materials. Do not pressure. The Personal Playbook audience includes people with legitimate privacy concerns, and coercing account creation would undermine the trust you're trying to build.

**If someone is struggling:** Walk to their device. Sometimes all it takes is someone else pressing the first button. Pair them with a neighbor. The buddy system works here exactly as it does in the business modules.

### Participants Use Real Personal Situations — This Is Intentional

In every business module, facilitators work with de-identified or fictional scenarios to protect PHI and client data. The Personal Playbook inverts this. Participants should use their real situations.

> "Don't use a fake example. Use your real week. Your real resume. Your actual situation. The prompt will be twice as useful when the context is real."

This instruction is repeated throughout the Personal Playbook facilitator guide because it is the mechanism of success. The brain dump prompt works because it's actually their messy Sunday-night task list, not a fictional person's. The resume tailoring works because it's their actual resume against a job description they actually want. The hard conversation prep works because it's the actual conversation they've been dreading for three weeks.

Encourage this explicitly for every use case. If a participant is using a generic placeholder ("imagine I have a job interview..."), gently redirect: "Do you have any upcoming conversations or interviews even loosely in mind? Real context will give you a more useful output."

### The Privacy Guardrail for Personal Data

The privacy guardrail for the Personal Playbook is different from the PHI protocol in the business modules. It is not about HIPAA. It is about personal data protection.

Give this briefing before participants start entering their own information. Make it matter-of-fact, not alarming:

```
Quick note before you start entering your own information — and this applies
every time you use AI tools. Consumer AI tools like ChatGPT free are not
the place for truly sensitive information. The rule I use: if losing it would
be catastrophic, don't paste it in. Specifically:

  — No Social Security numbers
  — No full financial account numbers
  — No medical information combined with your full name and date of birth
    (you can describe a situation generally without including your full identity)
  — No passwords

For everything in today's session, you can be real and specific without being
fully identified. Your resume without your home address. Your financial situation
described in ranges. A medical topic without your full name attached. You'll get
just as useful an output — and you've protected yourself.
```

**Where to show the privacy setting:** Right after this briefing, pull up ChatGPT Settings → Data Controls → "Improve the model for everyone" → Off. Run it on your device. Say: "This takes thirty seconds and it's worth doing right now." Participants who see you do it will do it themselves.

### The "Smart Friend" Demo Style

The Personal Playbook is delivered in first person. This is not optional.

The entire frame of the session — "AI as your smart friend" — only works if the smart friend is actually in the room. Teacher Scott's demos should feel personal and real, not scripted or corporate. The brain dump prompt uses your actual week's tasks when possible. The hard conversation demo uses a real scenario (or a real-feeling fictional one) that resonates because it sounds like someone's life, not a case study.

**Three designated personal story moments in the Personal Playbook:**

These are the three places where Teacher Scott shares from her own life. Not from the curriculum. From her life.

1. **Opening (Slide 1, before clicking Begin):** Share one thing you personally had on your plate when you first started using AI for your personal life — not for KEDAL, not for curriculum design, but for your own life. A decision you were avoiding. A conversation you were dreading. Something you finally got done. Frame: *"When I was going through my own career transition, I had this massive mental load of things I needed to figure out — and I kept telling myself I didn't have time. When I actually started using AI as my thinking partner for personal stuff, not just work stuff, that backlog started clearing. That's what today is about."*

2. **Hard Conversation slide (Slide 8 / P4):** This is the most important personal story moment in the session. Share a real conversation you prepared for with AI. Ideally something that surprised you — either by how well the prep worked, or by something AI surfaced that you hadn't considered. Frame: *"I've used this for a conversation about a family responsibility that I'd been avoiding for a long time. Writing out what I wanted to accomplish before the conversation changed how I walked into it. I wasn't just hoping it would go okay — I had a structure. That made a real difference."*

3. **Close (Slide 22):** Say the final send-off not as a trainer, but as a friend. *"I'm genuinely excited for you all. Not because you sat through a session — but because some of you are about to try something tonight that surprises you. Come back and tell me about it."*

These moments are not decorations. They are the reason this module works for non-corporate audiences. Leave your corporate trainer hat at the door. Today you are Kechia first, Teacher Scott second.

### The Follow-Up Habit: The 7-Day Starter Plan

Include this in every post-session follow-up email for the Personal Playbook. It is the bridge between session and habit. Send it within 24–48 hours.

The goal: one use case per day for a week. By Day 7, AI has shown up in enough different parts of their life that it starts to feel natural rather than special.

| Day | Use Case | The One Prompt to Try |
|---|---|---|
| **Monday** | Brain Dump | Paste your week into ChatGPT and ask for a structured plan with a Monday morning starting point |
| **Tuesday** | Email That Writes Itself | Find an email you've been putting off. Use the email prompt. Send it. |
| **Wednesday** | Research Without the Rabbit Hole | Look up one thing you've been meaning to research. Use the research prompt — not Google. |
| **Thursday** | Resume or LinkedIn | Update your resume summary or LinkedIn headline using the career prompt |
| **Friday** | Summarize Anything | Find one long document in your life — a policy, a lease, an email thread. Summarize it. |
| **Saturday** | Learn Something | Pick one topic you've wanted to understand better. Ask AI to teach you in 15 minutes. |
| **Sunday** | Hard Conversation Prep | Think of one conversation you've been avoiding. Run the prep prompt. You don't have to have the conversation yet — just get ready. |

Include the three guardrails as a reminder alongside the plan:
- AI drafts, you decide
- Specific beats vague
- Verify anything important

Pair the 7-day plan with a note that feels like it came from a friend, not from a training department: *"Pick one thing this week. Just one. The people who get the most out of this are the people who keep going — not the ones who did it perfectly the first time."*

### PHI Protocol Reminder (Business Modules Only)

**Read this aloud at the start of every business module hands-on section:**

> "Do not enter real patient information into any AI tool today — no patient names, MRNs, dates of birth, diagnosis codes, or claim data. Use your real operational metrics (no-show rates, volumes, cycle times — these are safe). For patient scenarios, we create realistic fictional patients together. Same rule as your email policy: you wouldn't send a patient's chart via personal Gmail. Same logic here."

**Three data tiers:**
- Aggregate operational metrics → Safe
- De-identified or fictional scenarios → Safe
- PHI or identified patient data → Never in consumer tools

**If a participant enters PHI:** Approach individually, calmly. Stop the input. Don't make it a room-wide moment. Redirect to a new chat thread with fictional details.

---

### Demo Protocol (7 Rules — In Order)

1. **Pre-type everything** — never cold-type a prompt live
2. **Narrate the input** before you paste — explain the role, context, constraints
3. **Read the output out loud** — don't let participants read silently
4. **Point before you explain** — let them see it before you tell them what to see
5. **Iterate once** — every demo must include at least one follow-up prompt
6. **Ask before you debrief** — "What do you notice?" before any commentary
7. **Four-minute maximum** — if running long, cut to the output and debrief

---

### Debrief Questions (Use 1–2 per task)

| Question | When to use |
|---|---|
| "What did you notice?" | Opening; most tasks |
| "What would you change about the prompt?" | After weak or mediocre output |
| "Where in your actual work would you use this?" | After a strong, resonant output |
| "What surprised you?" | Early tasks; mindset shift moments |

**If the output was bad:** Ask them to read their prompt. Diagnose which element was missing. Invite the room to suggest the fix. Try the revised version if time allows. This is the most valuable debrief in the session.

**Time per debrief:** 2–3 minutes. Cap it. Move on.

### Room Management Reminders

- **Pace gap:** Have follow-up challenges ready for every task (projected or printed)
- **Stuck participant:** Approach from the side, not front; ask "what are you thinking about starting with?" not "do you need help?"
- **Skeptic who won't try:** Don't force; pair with engaged participant; find the task closest to their specific pain
- **Off-topic rabbit hole:** Redirect, don't shut down; "let's finish this task and try that one after"
- **"My output was different":** Elevate it; compare the two outputs; ask what in the prompt caused the difference
- **20+ participants:** Allow 30–60 extra seconds per task; use table groups for debrief; circulate continuously

### Personal Playbook Reminders

- Account setup is a formal 5-minute session block, not pre-session overhead
- Participants use their **real** personal situations — this is intentional and required for the session to work
- Privacy guardrail: no SSNs, no full account numbers, no medical info tied to full name+DOB — use ranges and descriptions
- Three personal story moments: opening, hard conversation slide (P4), close — these are not optional
- Post-session: send the 7-Day Starter Plan within 24–48 hours
