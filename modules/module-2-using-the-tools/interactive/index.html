Why most people give up on AI

Three reasons AI disappoints

When people try AI and walk away frustrated, it almost always comes down to one of these.

01

Too little context

They gave AI a vague request and got a vague result. The tool isn't the problem — the input is.

"Write me an email" → Generic template that needs total rewrite
02

Accepted the first output

They treated AI like a vending machine. One press, take what comes out. AI is a conversation.

First draft → Final product → Disappointing result
03

Wrong tool for the task

ChatGPT for real-time research. Free tier for PHI. General model for clinical documentation.

Right task, wrong tool → Unreliable output

This module fixes all three. By the end, you'll have a repeatable framework and 12 real outputs you built yourself.

The framework

Anatomy of a strong prompt

Every effective prompt is built from some combination of these five elements.

R

Role

Who should AI be in this conversation?

"You are a healthcare compliance officer with 15 years of experience…"
T

Task

What specific action do you want?

"Write a 3-paragraph summary…" / "Generate a list of…" / "Review this and flag…"
C

Context

Background information AI needs to do the task well

"This is for a 450-bed regional hospital preparing for a Joint Commission audit…"
F

Format

How should the output be structured?

"Use bullet points." / "Write as a professional email." / "Organize by priority."
C

Constraints

What should AI NOT do? What limits apply?

"Keep it under 200 words." / "Do not include legal advice." / "Avoid jargon."

Minimum viable prompt for most tasks: Task + Context. Add the others when you need more precision.

Prompt quality — live comparison

The same task at four levels

Prior authorization appeal letter. Watch what changes as the prompt gets stronger.

"Write a prior authorization appeal letter."
Generic template. Heavy rewrite needed.
Level 2
"Write a prior auth appeal for a patient denied coverage for a continuous glucose monitor."
Better. Still no clinical specifics. Won't win an appeal.
Level 3
"Write a prior auth appeal for a 58-year-old with Type 2 diabetes denied CGM coverage. Denial: 'not medically necessary.' Include: ADA 2024 criteria, 3 ER visits in past 12 months, cost-effectiveness argument. Format as physician letter, under one page."
Clinically specific. Ready for physician review with minor edits.
Level 4
"You are a medical appeals specialist. Write a prior auth appeal… [full role + task + context + format + constraints]"
Submit-ready with minimal physician review. Structured, credible, specific.

The skill most courses skip

How to know if you can trust the output

AI output is formatted like truth. That's the problem.

01

Is it factually accurate?

For any claim that matters — clinical standards, legal requirements, financial figures — verify against a primary source. Do not assume.

02

Is it complete?

AI often produces outputs that look complete but are missing a critical piece. Did it include the specific clinical criteria? The enforcement section?

03

Is the tone right?

AI defaults to professional-but-generic. Does this need to sound like your organization? Your physician? Your leadership? Adjust it.

04

What didn't you ask for?

AI sometimes adds caveats, disclaimers, or sections you didn't request. Review and remove anything that doesn't belong.

🚩 Always verify before using when output contains:
Specific statistics or figures Regulation names or section numbers Clinical guidelines Named individuals or contacts "Current" or "as of" claims
Task 01

Hands-on practice

The Bad Prompt Fix

Start with failure. Then fix it.

1

Run this prompt exactly as written

"Help me with my email."

Observe the output. Note what's wrong with it.

2

Diagnose it using the 5 elements

Which elements are missing? Role? Task? Context? Format? Constraints?

3

Rewrite the prompt with all 5 elements

"You are a professional business writer. Draft a follow-up email to a hospital department head who attended our AI readiness presentation last week but hasn't responded to our proposal. Tone: warm but confident. Under 150 words. Include a specific call to action: schedule a 30-minute call this week."
4

Challenge

Ask AI: "Give me three versions of this — formal, conversational, and urgent." Compare all three.

Task 07

Communication prep

The Objection Handler

Use AI to anticipate resistance — and practice your responses.

Step 1: Generate objections

"I'm presenting an AI adoption proposal to a hospital CFO who is skeptical about ROI and worried about data security. Generate 5 likely objections they will raise, and for each, write a confident, evidence-based response I can use. Keep each response under 100 words."

Step 2: Role-play the conversation

"Now role-play as the skeptical CFO and push back on my responses. I'll respond and you keep challenging me."
This is one of the most powerful use cases in the module. AI as a sparring partner — building confidence before the real meeting.
Variation: Swap "hospital CFO" for any stakeholder your learners actually face — a compliance officer, a skeptical department head, a budget committee.

Task 11

The critical skill

The Fact-Check Exercise

AI formats everything like truth. Your job is to question it.

Setup

Teacher Scott will provide a paragraph of AI-generated content about a specific healthcare regulation. It contains 2–3 planted errors.

Your job

Read it carefully. Identify anything that seems off. Verify against the primary source.

What to look for

Wrong regulation numbers

AI confidently cites "45 CFR Part 164" when it should be Part 164.502. The format looks right. The number is wrong.

Outdated figures

HIPAA penalty tiers were updated. AI may cite the old amounts. Looks authoritative. Is wrong.

Plausible clinical claims

A clinical guideline cited correctly by name — but with the wrong recommendation. The most dangerous error type.

💬 Teaching point: "The more confident it sounds, the more carefully you should verify. Uncertainty-sounding output is easier to catch. It's the polished, authoritative-sounding output that gets people in trouble."
