## HOW TO USE THIS GUIDE

This guide is for Teacher Scott's eyes only. It maps directly to the 18-slide interactive module. For each slide, you'll find: what to say, what to do, what to watch out for, and how to handle the room. Keep this open on a second screen while presenting.

## PRE-SESSION CHECKLIST

**Technology:**
- [ ] Interactive module loaded and tested on the presentation device
- [ ] Screen mirroring or display cable connected and confirmed
- [ ] At least one AI tool open in a second browser tab (ChatGPT GPT-4o recommended for broad audiences)
- [ ] A domain-specific demo prompt pre-written and ready (see Demo Prompts section below)
- [ ] Audio working if you're playing any tool demos with sound

**Room:**
- [ ] Know your audience makeup (any executives in the room? clinical staff? HR leaders? compliance?)
- [ ] Know the client's primary pain points going in — what's the chore they hate?
- [ ] Have water. You're talking for a while.

**Mindset:**
- This session should feel like a conversation, not a lecture
- The best moments come from letting the room react to demos, not from explaining the demo
- Your experience in healthcare staffing is your credibility. Use it.

## SLIDE-BY-SLIDE NOTES

### Slide 1 — Cover
**Before you click Begin:**
Don't start with the slides. Start with a question:

> "When was the last time you did something at work where you thought — 'I can't believe we still do this manually'?"

Let two or three people answer. Write their answers down (flip chart or whiteboard). Tell them: "We're going to come back to these before the end of today."

Then click Begin.

---

### Slide 2 — Agenda
**What to say:**
"Today has two parts. The first half is going to challenge how you think about what your business is worth and what you're paying for. The second half is going to give you a framework for what to do about it."

"I want questions throughout. Especially the uncomfortable ones."

---

### Slide 3 — The Diamond Analogy
**This is the most important slide in the module. Spend real time here.**

**What to say:**
"If diamonds suddenly became like sand — available to everyone, everywhere, cheap to get — what would happen to the price of diamonds?"

Let the room answer. Then: "The same thing has happened to a wide category of skilled work. Not because the work doesn't matter. But because AI has collapsed the cost of producing it."

"This changes what things are worth. It changes what you charge. It changes what you should be paying for. And it changes what your competitors can do that they couldn't do before."

**Watch for:** Some people will get it immediately. Others will resist. Don't chase the resisters yet — that's what slides 9 and 10 are for.

---

### Slide 4 — What This Means
**What to say:**
"I want to be direct here. If any part of your business model is built on the idea that something is hard to do — and AI can now do it cheaply — you have a problem. Not eventually. Now."

"That's not a threat. It's an opportunity — if you move first."

**Discussion prompt (optional):**
"Think about the tasks your team does every day. Which ones are valuable because they're hard? Which ones are valuable because they're right?"

---

### Slides 5–8 — Domain Examples
**Your job on these slides: show, don't tell.**

For each section, pick ONE example that's most relevant to the audience in the room. Then open the live tool and do it.

**Recommended demo sequence by audience:**

| Audience | Best demo | Tool | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Healthcare ops team | Prior auth appeal letter from bullet points | ChatGPT GPT-4o | 2 min |
| HR/Staffing team | Specialty nursing job description | ChatGPT GPT-4o | 90 sec |
| Leadership/exec | Summarize a policy doc, flag top 3 risks | Claude or Perplexity | 3 min |
| Compliance team | Draft a HIPAA training module intro | ChatGPT Enterprise | 2 min |
| Mixed audience | Turn a rough agenda into a project brief | ChatGPT GPT-4o | 2 min |

**After the demo, ask:**
"What do you notice?" — Don't explain the output. Let them react first.

### Slide 9 — Hallucinations
**Critical: validate before you answer.**

Say: "That's exactly the right question — I'd be worried about anyone who didn't ask it."

Then work through the four points on screen. The key framing is:

> "AI doesn't eliminate the need for professional judgment. It changes where that judgment is applied — from generating the first draft to reviewing it."

**The human analogy:**
"How many of you have reviewed a report or a letter written by a human colleague and found errors? That's the same workflow. AI just produces the first draft faster."

**On domain-specific models:**
"Nuance DAX is trained on millions of clinical encounters. It is dramatically less likely to hallucinate a clinical term than a general-purpose model. This is why matching the tool to the task matters."

**On prompting to reduce hallucination:**
"Ask AI to cite its sources. Ask it to show its reasoning. Ask it to flag where it's uncertain. These are all things you can prompt for."

---

### Slide 10 — Security
**Don't minimize this. Executives will disengage if it's hand-waved.**

Walk through the tier table row by row. The key message:

> "The tool isn't insecure. Using the wrong tier for the wrong use case is what creates risk. This is not different from your email policy — you don't send PHI over personal Gmail. Same logic applies here."

**The policy extension framing:**
"Most of you already have a data classification policy. PHI lives here, internal docs live there, public info lives somewhere else. You need to extend that policy to AI tools. That's the whole ask."

**Pro move:**
"Most of your employees are already using consumer AI tools. They're using the free tier of ChatGPT at home and at work. The question isn't 'will AI be used' — it's 'will it be used correctly, with the right guardrails?' That starts with leadership naming it."

---

### Slide 11 — 3P Framework
**Transition energy. Shift from problem to solution.**

Read the three wrong ways out loud. Ask: "Raise your hand if your organization has done one of these." Almost every room will have at least two or three hands go up for each.

Then reveal the three P's cleanly. Don't over-explain yet. Just name them and promise to go deep.

---

### Slide 12 — Products
**Live demo moment #2 (if you haven't done one yet).**

If the audience hasn't seen a demo yet, this is the moment. Pick one tool from the tool grid on screen that this specific audience uses — or should use — and do it live.

**The procurement question:**
Tell them: "Make this a standard question in every vendor review going forward: 'Does this have an AI-infused version? What are you doing with AI?' If the answer is nothing, that's a red flag."

---

### Slide 13 — People / Bandwidth
**This is the emotional center of the whole training.**

Let the bandwidth ladder sit on screen while you talk. Point to "Access" — "Most of you are here. You have the login. You haven't had the time."

"And this is the most common failure mode I see. Organizations treat AI like software rollout. Buy the licenses, send the login, done. That is not how fluency works. That is not how your team will ever get to a point where AI is changing how they work."

**The practice analogy:**
"Think about learning any professional skill — clinical skills, project management, even typing. You don't get better by having access to the tool. You get better by practicing with it, repeatedly, on real work, with enough time to make mistakes and iterate."

---

### Slide 14 — ROI Calc
**For the CFO or COO in the room.**

Walk through the math slowly. These numbers are conservative and based on a $60K salary. If you know the audience's salary range, adjust in real time.

"The cost of giving people 2 hours a week for 3 months is trivially small. You would never buy a $150,000 piece of medical equipment and not budget for training. AI tools are the same."

---

### Slide 15 — Process
**Sequence is everything here.**

"Products first, then People, then Process. In that order. If you redesign your workflows before your people have fluency, you'll mandate a process no one has the skills to execute."

Walk through the five steps. Ask: "Who here has ever mapped a workflow and been surprised by how it actually works versus how you thought it worked?" Most ops people will raise their hands.

---

### Slide 16 — Prior Auth Example
**Walk through this slowly. Every healthcare ops person in the room has lived this.**

Ask: "Who has auth coordinators on your team? What does their week look like?" Let them describe it. Then walk through the before/after.

The critical reframe:
"AI doesn't replace the coordinator. AI eliminates the 45-minute writing task. The coordinator can now handle 4 to 5 times the case volume. The role survives — the chore doesn't."

---

### Slide 17 — Change Management
**Human tone. This is where resistance lives.**

Read each pushback out loud. Ask if the room has heard them. Most will have.

**On "this will eliminate jobs":**
"I want to be direct about this one, because it's the fear underneath every other objection. AI will eliminate tasks within jobs. Repetitive, frustrating, low-judgment tasks. The roles that will be most at risk are the ones that refuse to adapt — not because AI replaced them, but because their competition adapted and they didn't."

"The question for leaders is: do you want your team to fear this transition, or be equipped for it? Because that is a choice."

---

### Slide 18 — Close
**Bring energy back to the room.**

Return to the answers from your opening question — "you said prior auth letters, meeting notes, scheduling changes." Walk through which week of the 30-day plan would address each one.

End with the tagline out loud:
"The organizations that win won't be the ones with the best AI. They'll be the ones who learned first."

Ask for a show of hands: "Which of these four weeks can you commit to starting Monday?"

## DEMO PROMPTS LIBRARY

### Prompt 1: Prior Auth Appeal Letter
**Use for:** Healthcare ops, clinical staff
**Tool:** ChatGPT GPT-4o
```
Write a prior authorization appeal letter for a 58-year-old patient with Type 2 diabetes and peripheral neuropathy who was denied coverage for a continuous glucose monitor. The denial was based on "not medically necessary." Include clinical criteria referencing ADA standards of care, patient functional limitations, and cost-effectiveness of CGM over ER visits from hypoglycemic episodes. Format it as a professional letter from the treating physician.
```

### Prompt 2: Specialty Nursing Job Description
**Use for:** HR, staffing, workforce teams
**Tool:** ChatGPT GPT-4o
```
Write a detailed job description for a Level II Trauma ICU Registered Nurse at a 450-bed regional medical center. Include required certifications (CCRN preferred, BLS/ACLS required), key responsibilities, physical requirements, preferred experience, and a brief section on the unit culture and growth opportunities. Tone should be professional but welcoming to new grads as well as experienced nurses.
```

### Prompt 3: Policy Summary with Risk Flags
**Use for:** Leadership, compliance, legal
**Tool:** Perplexity or Claude
```
Summarize the following [paste policy text or URL] and identify the top 3 compliance risks for a healthcare organization. For each risk, suggest one practical mitigation action.
```

### Prompt 4: Meeting Agenda → Project Brief
**Use for:** Mixed audience, operations teams
**Tool:** ChatGPT GPT-4o
```
Turn this rough meeting agenda into a structured project brief with an objective, key stakeholders, success metrics, and a 30-60-90 day milestone plan:

[Agenda: Q3 EHR optimization kickoff — discuss current scheduling inefficiencies, review Epic upgrade timeline, assign workstream leads, agree on communication cadence]
```

### Prompt 5: HIPAA Training Module Intro
**Use for:** Compliance, HR, leadership
**Tool:** ChatGPT Enterprise or Claude for Enterprise
```
Write the introduction section for a 30-minute HIPAA refresher training for healthcare administrative staff. Include: why HIPAA matters in everyday work, the top 3 mistakes admin staff make with PHI, and a brief scenario-based warm-up question to engage learners before the main content.
```

## HANDLING COMMON OBJECTIONS

### "Our IT department won't allow this."
**Don't fight IT. Recruit them.**
"That's the right instinct from IT. They're protecting you. What I'd ask is: have you had a conversation with IT specifically about the enterprise and HIPAA-eligible tier options? Because those were designed for exactly this concern. IT can own the security tier policy — that's actually a good role for them."

### "We don't have budget for new tools."
"Most of your team is already paying for Microsoft 365. Copilot is an add-on to what you already have. And some of the most powerful tools — Perplexity, Claude's free tier, basic ChatGPT — are free to start. The question isn't budget. It's permission and time."

### "We tried it and the output was garbage."
"I'd love to know what you tried it for, because that usually tells the story. If someone handed a first-year employee the task with no context, no template, and no guidance, the output would be garbage too. AI works best when you give it structure. What was the task?"

### "How do I know what's true in the output?"
"Same way you'd know with any document — you review it. AI is a first-draft generator, not a final-answer oracle. The professional judgment doesn't go away. It shifts. You spend less time on production and more time on verification and refinement. For most knowledge workers, that's actually a better use of their skills."

### "Our patients/clients won't trust AI."
"Your patients don't need to know which tool drafted the note — they need to know the note is accurate and the care is good. Every clinician uses tools that support their work. AI is a tool. The clinician is still responsible. The relationship is still human."
