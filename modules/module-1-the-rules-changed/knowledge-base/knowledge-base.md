## PART 1 — THE FOUNDATION: "THE RULES CHANGED"

### Core Thesis

> "Things that were expensive are now cheap. Things that were valuable are no longer as valuable, because things that were expensive are cheap. If diamonds suddenly became like sand — available to everyone — no one would pay a lot for diamonds anymore."

This is not a metaphor about technology. It is a statement about **economic value**. AI has collapsed the cost of production for a wide range of skilled tasks that previously required significant time, expertise, or money. When the cost of production collapses, the market value of those outputs collapses with it.

**The implication for every business:**
Any part of your business model, pricing structure, workflow, or competitive advantage that was built on the *scarcity* of a skill or the *cost* of a task is now at risk. Not eventually — now.

---

### The Diamond-to-Sand Shift: What Just Happened

Before AI, many business tasks had high value because they had high cost:
- Writing a proposal took hours from a skilled person → proposals cost money to produce → you charged accordingly or rationed them
- Creating a job description required HR expertise → it took time → you produced fewer of them
- Building a training curriculum required instructional design knowledge → it was expensive → organizations bought it from vendors
- Generating a market analysis required a researcher → it took days → only well-resourced organizations could afford it
- Drafting a contract required a lawyer → $300-$500/hour → access was limited by budget

AI has changed the **input cost** of all of the above. Not eliminated them — changed them. The question every business must now answer is: **what does that mean for how we operate, what we charge, and where we compete?**

## THE TWO OBJECTIONS EVERY ROOM WILL RAISE

### Objection 1: "What About Hallucinations?"

**What they mean:** AI makes things up. How do I trust it?

**The honest answer:**
Yes. AI language models can produce confident-sounding content that is factually wrong. This is real. It is not a reason to avoid AI — it is a reason to use AI correctly.

**The framework for handling hallucinations:**

1. **AI as a first draft, not a final product.** Every AI output is a starting point. The human is the reviewer. The professional judgment doesn't go away — it shifts from generation to verification.

2. **Domain-specific models reduce hallucination risk.** A general-purpose model writing a clinical note has more room to err than a model trained on clinical data (e.g., Nuance DAX, which is trained on millions of clinical encounters). Match the tool to the task.

3. **Structured outputs reduce hallucination.** When you give AI a specific format to follow, a source to summarize, or a template to fill, hallucination risk drops significantly. Open-ended generation is where the risk is highest.

4. **Hallucination is not new — it just has a new name.** Junior employees make things up too. Consultants pad reports. The difference is that AI can produce a wrong answer at scale, fast. Your job as a leader is to build the review process into the workflow, not to eliminate AI because mistakes are possible.

**Teaching moment:** Ask the room — "How many of you have reviewed a document written by a human and found errors?" That's the same workflow. AI just drafts faster.

**Tool callout:** Show how to prompt AI to cite its sources, show its reasoning, or flag uncertainty. Models like Perplexity are designed specifically to show sources. Claude tends to flag its own uncertainty. ChatGPT can be prompted to do the same.

---

### Objection 2: "What About Security?"

**What they mean:** If I put our patient data, proprietary information, or employee records into ChatGPT, what happens to it?

**The honest answer:**
This is the right question to ask, and it has a real answer. The answer depends entirely on *which* tool you use and *how* you configure it.

**The security spectrum — teach this framework:**

| Tier | Examples | Data handling | Appropriate for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Consumer free tier | ChatGPT free, Claude free | May be used for training data | Zero PHI, zero proprietary data |
| Consumer paid tier | ChatGPT Plus | Limited protections, not HIPAA-compliant | General drafting only, no sensitive data |
| Enterprise / API | ChatGPT Enterprise, Claude for Enterprise, Microsoft Copilot 365 | Data not used for training, enterprise SLAs | Business operations, internal documents |
| HIPAA-eligible | Microsoft Azure OpenAI, AWS Bedrock, Nuance DAX | BAA available, HIPAA-compliant configurations | Clinical documentation, PHI-adjacent workflows |
| On-premise / private | Self-hosted models (Llama, Mistral on private cloud) | Data never leaves your environment | Highest sensitivity: proprietary research, PII, PHI |

**The key message:** The tool is not inherently insecure. Using the wrong tier for the wrong use case is what creates risk. Your job as a leader is to establish a **data classification policy** that maps your data types to the appropriate AI tool tiers.

**Teaching moment:** Most organizations already have some version of this for email and file sharing. You don't send PHI over personal Gmail — same logic applies to AI tools. The policy already exists in spirit; it needs to be extended to AI.

**What to do right now:**
1. Inventory what AI tools your people are already using (they are — whether you know it or not)
2. Classify your data types (PHI, PII, proprietary, general)
3. Match each data type to an approved tool tier
4. Communicate the policy before you mandate the tools

## PART 2 — THE FRAMEWORK: PRODUCTS → PEOPLE → PROCESS

### The Core Insight

Most organizations respond to AI in one of three wrong ways:
1. **Ignore it** — "We'll wait and see." (Your competitors aren't waiting.)
2. **Over-invest in tools** — "We got everyone access to Copilot." (Then what?)
3. **Mandate without support** — "Use AI in your work." (How? When? For what?)

The 3P Framework gives organizations a sequenced, practical path.

---

### P1: PRODUCTS — Replace Your Tools with AI-Infused Versions

**The question to ask:** For every tool your team currently uses, ask: "Is there an AI-infused version of this?"

This is not about replacing everything at once. It's about being intentional in how you evaluate and adopt tools going forward.

**Categories to audit:**

| Category | Legacy tool | AI-infused alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Documentation | Word, Google Docs | Notion AI, Microsoft Copilot in Word |
| Communication | Email (manual) | Copilot in Outlook, AI email drafting |
| Scheduling | Manual calendar management | Reclaim.ai, Motion, QGenda (healthcare) |
| Meeting notes | Manual transcription / EA | Otter.ai, Fireflies, Microsoft Teams transcription |
| Research | Manual web search | Perplexity, ChatGPT, Consensus (for clinical) |
| Presentations | PowerPoint (manual) | Gamma, Beautiful.ai, Copilot in PowerPoint |
| Hiring | Manual ATS review | Paradox (Olivia), Findem, HireEZ |
| Clinical documentation | Manual EHR entry | Nuance DAX, Ambience, Suki AI |
| Compliance monitoring | Manual review cycles | Compliancy Group, Accountable AI |
| Contract management | Email + manual tracking | ContractPodAi, Ironclad, Harvey AI |

**Key teaching point:** You don't need to change every tool today. But you do need a process for evaluating new tools through an AI lens. Make "does this have an AI-infused version?" a standard procurement question.

**The tease:** Live demo opportunity — pull up one tool the audience likely uses (e.g., ChatGPT or Copilot) and show one task done in under 2 minutes that would normally take 30.

---

### P2: PEOPLE — Bandwidth Is Not Optional

**The core problem most organizations make:**

> "They go and get everybody access to the tools, but they don't give them the time it takes to sit down and use a ChatGPT for work. To get to a point where you're starting to do different things, you need the time."

This is the most common failure mode. Organizations treat AI adoption like software rollout: buy the licenses, send the login, done.

That is not how fluency works.

**The bandwidth framework:**

| Level | What it looks like | Time investment |
|---|---|---|
| Access | Employee has a login | 0 hours of practice |
| Awareness | Employee has seen a demo | 1-2 hours total |
| Experimentation | Employee has tried it on their own work | 5-10 hours |
| Fluency | Employee uses AI tools as a natural part of daily workflow | 20-40 hours of intentional practice |
| Amplification | Employee is redesigning their work around AI capabilities | Ongoing |

**Most organizations stop at Access or Awareness and wonder why adoption is low.**

**What giving people bandwidth actually looks like:**
- Protected time blocks (1-2 hours/week) dedicated to AI exploration — not deliverables, just learning
- Structured challenges: "This week, use AI to do one task you normally do manually"
- Peer sharing: 15-minute weekly standup where someone shares what they tried
- No-judgment zones: AI output doesn't have to be perfect; the point is practice
- Leadership modeling: If the executive isn't using it, the team won't trust that it's serious

**The ROI argument for skeptical executives:**
If a $60,000/year employee saves 5 hours per week using AI tools, that's 260 hours/year = $7,500 in labor value returned. The cost of giving them 2 hours/week of protected learning time for 3 months = 24 hours = $693. The return is 10x in year one.

---

### P3: PROCESS — Now You Reinvent How Work Gets Done

**This is where the transformation actually happens.**

Products + People only unlock value if they're followed by Process redesign. If you give people AI tools and bandwidth but keep every workflow the same, you've added a tool to a broken process. You haven't changed anything.

**The process reinvention cycle:**

1. **Map current state** — Document how work actually gets done today (not how the org chart says it gets done)
2. **Identify AI insertion points** — Where in this workflow is a human doing something AI can do faster/better/cheaper?
3. **Redesign the workflow** — Eliminate, automate, or augment each step. The human role shifts to judgment, review, and relationship.
4. **Pilot and measure** — Run the new process with one team before scaling. Measure time, quality, and adoption.
5. **Codify and scale** — Document the new process as the standard. Train everyone on it.

**Healthcare example — prior authorization workflow:**

*Before:*
Clinical staff receives denial → manually drafts appeal letter (45-90 min) → submits → waits → follows up manually

*After:*
Clinical staff receives denial → AI drafts appeal letter from clinical notes in 2 minutes → staff reviews and edits (5-10 min) → submits → AI monitors status and flags for follow-up

*Result:* Auth coordinator handles 4-5x more cases. Appeal quality improves (AI doesn't forget to include clinical criteria). Staff burnout from repetitive writing decreases.

**The role of change management in Process:**
Process redesign is where resistance lives. People don't resist AI tools — they resist change to their routines, their roles, and their sense of competence. The change management work is:
- Communicate the "why" before the "what"
- Involve the people doing the work in the redesign
- Name the fear directly: "This is not about eliminating your job. It's about eliminating the parts of your job that are making you miserable."
- Celebrate early wins publicly

## FACILITATOR NOTES

### Room Setup
- Have at least one live tool demo ready (ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Copilot)
- Pre-load a healthcare-specific example prompt — ideally relevant to the client's exact work
- Have the security tier table on a slide you can return to when the objection comes up (it always does)

### Pacing
- Diamond analogy: spend 10-15 minutes here. Let the room sit with it. Ask: "What in your organization right now is something you pay a lot for because it's hard to produce?" Then show them AI doing it.
- Hallucination objection: When it comes up (and it will), validate it first before you answer it. "That's the right question. Let me show you how to think about it."
- Security objection: Do not minimize it. Executives will disengage if they feel it's being hand-waved. Walk through the tier framework slowly.
- The 3P Framework: Introduce it as a whole, then go deep on each P. The room will immediately try to jump to "which tools" — hold them at People first, because that's where most orgs fail.

### Discussion Questions
1. "What task does your team do every week that feels like a chore — repetitive, time-consuming, not requiring real judgment?" (This is the first AI insertion point.)
2. "If I gave your team 2 hours a week to just experiment with AI tools, what would get in the way of that actually happening?"
3. "What would you need to see — what evidence — to feel comfortable that the AI output is trustworthy enough to act on?"
4. "What process in your organization has been the same for 5+ years and hasn't been questioned?"

### Live Demo Protocol
Pick ONE task relevant to the client's industry. Show the before (describe or show the manual process). Then show the AI doing it live. Do not over-explain. Let the output speak. Then ask: "What do you notice?"

Recommended demo tasks by audience:
- Healthcare ops team → draft a prior auth appeal letter from bullet points
- HR/staffing → generate a job description for a specialty nursing role in 90 seconds
- Leadership → ask AI to summarize a 10-page policy document and flag the top 3 compliance risks
- Mixed audience → ask AI to turn a rough meeting agenda into a structured project brief

### Handling Pushback
- "Our organization would never allow this" → "Tell me more about what specifically concerns leadership. Let's map it to the security framework."
- "We tried it and it was wrong" → "What did you use it for? Let's look at the task and the tool together."
- "This will eliminate jobs" → "It will eliminate tasks within jobs. The jobs that survive will be the ones that adapt. The risk isn't AI — it's being the organization that didn't learn."
- "We're too regulated" → "Regulated industries are where AI vendors are investing the most right now, because the ROI of reducing documentation burden is so high. Let me show you what's HIPAA-eligible."

## KEY TERMS GLOSSARY

**AI Fluency** — The ability to use AI tools effectively as a natural part of professional workflows. Not coding or technical expertise — the ability to prompt, evaluate, and apply AI outputs to real work.

**Hallucination** — When an AI model generates content that sounds confident and plausible but is factually incorrect. Mitigated by structured prompting, source verification, and domain-specific tools.

**Large Language Model (LLM)** — The underlying technology behind tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Copilot. Trained on large amounts of text to predict and generate language. Does not "know" things the way humans do — generates probabilistic outputs.

**HIPAA-eligible AI** — AI tools that have signed a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with healthcare organizations, meaning they meet HIPAA's technical and contractual requirements for handling Protected Health Information (PHI).

**Prompt** — The instruction or input you give an AI tool. The quality of the output is directly tied to the quality of the prompt.

**AI-infused tool** — An existing category of software (word processor, scheduling tool, EHR) that has incorporated AI capabilities into its core product.

**3P Framework** — Products → People → Process. The sequenced model for AI adoption in organizations. Must be done in order: tools first, fluency second, workflow redesign third.

**Bandwidth** — Protected time given to employees to learn, experiment with, and develop fluency in AI tools. The single most underinvested resource in AI adoption.

**Change management** — The structured approach to transitioning individuals, teams, and organizations from a current state to a desired future state. Critical for AI adoption because resistance is about people, not technology.
