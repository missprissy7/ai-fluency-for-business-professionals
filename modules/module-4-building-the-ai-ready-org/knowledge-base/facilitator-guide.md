Keep this guide open on a second screen. The tone shift from Modules 1–3 is intentional: this audience is leadership. They don't need to be convinced AI exists. They need a strategy, a governance framework, and a way to make the business case to the people above them and beside them.

## HANDS-ON DELIVERY SETUP

### Why M4 Is Different from M2 and M3

In Modules 2 and 3, hands-on work was individual: participants ran prompts for their own tasks, iterated on their own outputs, built their own use case maps. That format works for individual skill development.

Module 4 is organizational. The decisions being made are collective — governance policy affects everyone, a champion model only works if leadership backs it, ROI measurement requires agreement on what to measure. For these reasons, **the hands-on work in M4 is primarily group-based, not individual.**

There are still two live demos (the governance policy prompt and the business case prompt). These are teacher-led and high-impact. But the majority of the active work happens in table groups, culminating in the capstone blueprint.

### Three Delivery Options

Choose based on your read of the room before the session starts. You can also combine options across sections (e.g., Option A for Pillar 1, Option C for the capstone).

#### Option A — Observe and Discuss (Executive-Heavy Rooms)
**Best for:** Rooms where most participants are C-suite or VP-level; rooms where participants do not routinely use AI tools themselves; boards and governance committees

**How it works:** Teacher Scott runs all demos. Participants watch, react, and discuss. No prompts typed at individual seats. Group discussion questions are posed after each demo. The capstone blueprint is completed individually (not in groups) with light facilitation support.

**When to choose this:** When getting executives to type prompts on a laptop would create friction that overshadows the learning. Some executive audiences find individual seat-level tool use infantilizing — they'd rather see it done well and discuss implications. Read the room in the first 15 minutes.

**Trade-off:** Lower personal activation. Compensate with high-quality debrief questions that connect the demo directly to their specific strategic challenges.

---

#### Option B — Participants Run Prompts Live (Mixed Manager Rooms)
**Best for:** Director and manager-level audiences; participants who completed M2 and M3 hands-on and have built tool comfort; rooms where participants have laptops or devices

**How it works:** Teacher Scott runs the marquee demos (governance, business case) projected. For 2–3 additional moments in the session, participants are invited to adapt and run a prompt themselves at their seats using a scenario relevant to their own organization. Brief share-out follows.

**When to choose this:** When you want participants to leave with a prompt they built themselves — especially the business case prompt, which many will use within the week.

**Trade-off:** Pacing risk. Individual prompt-running can go long. Keep it tight: 5 minutes maximum per individual prompt activity.

---

#### Option C — Table Group Exercises (Recommended for the Capstone)
**Best for:** All Module 4 audiences for the capstone section; mixed leadership rooms with enough role diversity per table to have a real conversation

**How it works:** Participants are divided into table groups of 3–5. Each group is given a structured task — typically a section of the 90-Day Blueprint — applied to a fictional healthcare organization scenario that Teacher Scott provides. Groups work for 10–12 minutes, then one member presents back to the room. Debrief happens as a full group.

**When to choose this:** **This is the recommended format for the capstone in virtually all M4 deliveries.** Group work accomplishes what individual work cannot: it surfaces disagreement (which is productive), forces prioritization, and produces a richer artifact than any one person would build alone.

**Trade-off:** Requires pre-assigned groups and a well-structured scenario. Both are provided below.

### Slide 3 — Why AI Initiatives Fail (3 Failure Modes)

**What to say:**
"Let me name the three ways these initiatives die — because I've seen all three in healthcare organizations, and at least one of them is probably familiar to you."

Walk through each failure mode with a beat of silence before the next:

- **Top-down mandate without ground-level buy-in:** "Leadership announces 'we're doing AI.' Nobody explains to the people doing the work why it matters to them. Resistance builds quietly. By month three, nobody's using the tools."
- **Training without workflow integration:** "People come to training, they learn the tools, they go back to a role where nothing has changed. The skills have nowhere to land. The training investment evaporates."
- **No internal champions:** "Adoption depends entirely on the external trainer — on me, on whoever held the training — or on one enthusiastic leader. When that person's attention moves on, so does the initiative."

**Discussion prompt:**
"Which of these is the most dangerous for your organization right now? Not in general — for you, specifically."

Take 2–3 hands. Don't solve the problem yet. Hold it. "We're going to address all three today."

---

### Slide 4 — The Adoption Curve: Where Organizations Stall

**What to say:**
"Here's what adoption actually looks like in organizations — not the version vendors sell you, the version I see in the field."

Walk through the four stages:
- **Awareness (0–3 months):** "People know AI exists. They've seen a demo. They might have a login."
- **Experimentation (1–6 months):** "Individuals try tools for personal tasks. Results are inconsistent. Some people are excited, some are skeptical, most are neutral."
- **Workflow Integration (3–12 months):** "AI is embedded into recurring processes. Time savings are measurable. This is where it becomes real."
- **Organizational Capability (12+ months):** "AI literacy is part of how you hire, onboard, and evaluate performance. It's cultural."

"Most organizations are stuck between Experimentation and Workflow Integration. They've got the tools. They haven't changed the workflows. And the move from Experimentation to Integration doesn't happen through more training. It happens through deliberate structure — which is what we're building today."

**Debrief question:**
"Where would you honestly put your organization on this curve right now?"

### Slide 5 — The Internal Champion Model

**What to say:**
"The single most effective driver of AI adoption is not a top-down mandate. It's not an external trainer. It's peer influence. Someone your team already trusts showing them something that works."

Describe the champion profile:
- "They don't have to be the most senior person. They have to be respected by their peers."
- "They've personally experienced AI value in their own work — not heard about it, experienced it."
- "They're given time and recognition to share what they've learned."
- "They own a specific department or function, not the whole organization. That's intentional — champions don't scale by trying to cover everything."

**Discussion prompt:**
"Who is that person in your organization right now? Not who should be — who actually is. And if you don't know, that's a gap."

**Watch for:** Some executive rooms will respond to this with "that's me." Affirm it — but gently push back: "Good. You need more than one. And the ones below you need to see peers doing this, not just leadership."

### Slide 7 — The 30-60-90 Adoption Plan

**What to say:**
"This is the structural answer to the workflow integration problem. Not a policy, not a mandate — a sequenced plan that's specific enough to actually execute."

Walk through each phase:

- **Days 1–30:** "Individual habit formation. Each person identifies one recurring task, commits to running that prompt at least three times in the first month, and tracks time saved. It doesn't have to be sophisticated. A Post-it note count works."
- **Days 31–60:** "Team integration. Identify two or three workflows in the team that involve the most templated, information-handling work. Map where AI can accelerate. Run one internal show-and-tell where early adopters share what's working."
- **Days 61–90:** "Process redesign. Rebuild at least one end-to-end workflow with AI embedded at multiple steps. Document it. Use AI to write the SOP — that's not lazy, that's efficient."

"Notice the sequence: individual first, then team, then process. If you try to redesign the workflow before your people have the fluency, you'll mandate a process nobody has the skills to execute."

**[GROUP EXERCISE — Optional, 5 min]**
If time allows: "Turn to your table. You have four minutes. Pick one workflow in your organization — something that touches multiple people and has a lot of templated, information-handling work. Which phase of this 30-60-90 plan would it land in? What would have to be true to move it to the next phase?"

Brief share-out: ask one table to share.

### Slide 8 — Managing Resistance: The 4 Objections

**What to say:**
"You're going to hear these. I want you to have language ready — not because you're going to argue, but because hesitation deserves a real answer."

Work through each objection:

**"AI will take my job"**
> "This is the fear underneath every other objection. Here's the honest answer: AI changes what your job requires, not whether your job exists. The people who learn this become more valuable. The roles that will be at risk are the ones that refuse to adapt — not because AI replaced them, but because their competition adapted and they didn't. The question for leaders is: do you want your team to fear this transition, or be equipped for it? That is a choice."

**"I don't trust the output"**
> "Good. You shouldn't trust it blindly. Module 2 was entirely about output evaluation. The skill isn't trusting AI — it's knowing when to verify and how. AI produces a draft, not a decision."

**"This is too complicated"**
> "You don't need to understand how it works. You need to know how to use it. You know how to use your phone's GPS without understanding satellite triangulation. Same principle."

**"We already tried it and it didn't work"**
> "What specifically didn't work? Most early failures come from using the wrong tool for the task, writing vague prompts, or not knowing how to evaluate the output. Those are all solvable. Let's look at what happened."

**Watch for:** Executive rooms often don't voice these objections out loud — they carry them internally. You may need to surface them directly: "I want to name some things you may be hearing from your teams, or thinking yourselves."

### Slide 10 — The Three-Tier Security Model

**What to say:**
"AI tools are not all the same. The single most important thing your organization can do right now is establish a clear policy about which tier is appropriate for which data. Because your staff are already using these tools — most of them without a policy guiding them."

Walk through each tier:

**Tier 1 — Consumer Free Tools (ChatGPT free, Gemini free, Claude free):**
"Data may be used for model training. No BAA available. Appropriate for: public information, internal brainstorming with no PII or PHI, personal learning. Not appropriate for: anything with patient data, employee PII, financial data, proprietary business information."

**Tier 2 — Consumer Paid / Team Plans (ChatGPT Plus, Claude Pro):**
"Data is not used for training by default. Still no BAA. Appropriate for: de-identified operational data, general business tasks with no regulated data. Not appropriate for: PHI, employee SSNs, credentialing files, payroll data."

**Tier 3 — Enterprise / HIPAA-Eligible (ChatGPT Enterprise, Microsoft Copilot M365, Azure OpenAI with BAA):**
"BAA available. Data isolated to your organization's tenant. Appropriate for: PHI with proper configuration, HR data, financial records. Requires: IT configuration, BAA execution, staff training on approved use."

"The tool isn't insecure. Using the wrong tier for the wrong data is what creates risk. This is not different from your email policy — you don't send PHI over personal Gmail. The logic is identical."

**The key framing:**
> "Most of your employees are already using consumer AI tools at work. The question isn't 'will AI be used' — it's 'will it be used with the right guardrails?' That starts with leadership naming it."

### Slide 11 — HIPAA + AI: The Intersection

**What to say:**
"Let me walk you through the four HIPAA principles that apply directly to AI use — because your compliance officer is going to ask about these, and you need to be able to answer."

**Minimum Necessary Standard:**
"Just because an AI tool can process an entire patient record doesn't mean it should. Staff should use the minimum PHI necessary for the task — same standard as any other PHI disclosure."

**Business Associate Agreements:**
"Any AI vendor that processes, creates, or transmits PHI on behalf of a covered entity is a Business Associate. BAAs must be in place before PHI enters that system. The most common compliance violation I see: staff using free consumer tools with patient data before a BAA exists."

**Training Documentation:**
"HIPAA requires documented training for staff who access PHI. AI-specific training — what tools are approved, what data may be used, how to report incidents — should be added to your existing HIPAA training documentation. This is not optional."

**Breach Risk Assessment:**
"The four-factor breach risk assessment applies to AI incidents just as it applies to email misdirection. If a staff member enters PHI into an unapproved tool, that is an incident. You need a documented process for evaluating it."

**Pro move:**
If the organization has had an incident — even a minor one — this is where it surfaces. You don't have to name it. Just say: "If you've had something happen that felt like it might have been an issue — staff entering patient data somewhere they shouldn't have — this is the framework for evaluating it."

### Slide 12 — Building Governance Infrastructure: 5 Steps

**What to say:**
"Here's the sequenced path to building your governance infrastructure. Five steps. This is doable in 90 days if you have the right people assigned."

Walk through each step:

1. **Inventory (Week 1):** "Survey your staff — or even just your department heads — on what AI tools they're currently using. Every organization I've worked with is surprised by the range of tools already in use without policy."

2. **Policy Draft (Weeks 2–3):** "Use the governance prompt we're going to run in a moment to draft your Acceptable Use Policy. Then hand it to legal and compliance for review. You're giving them a structured starting point, not asking them to build from scratch."

3. **Communicate (Week 4):** "Distribute the policy — but don't just post it to the intranet. Run a brief all-staff communication that explains why the policy exists and what it protects. Emphasize: this protects your staff, not just the organization."

4. **Training (Month 2):** "Add AI governance to new hire orientation and annual HIPAA training. Document it."

5. **Monitor & Update (Ongoing):** "Designate an owner. Set a quarterly review date. AI tools evolve faster than annual policy cycles. This policy needs to be a living document."

### Slide 15 — What ROI Looks Like: 3 Categories

**What to say:**
"AI ROI in healthcare organizations falls into three buckets, and they're not all equally easy to measure."

**Time Savings — most measurable:**
"Identify a recurring task, baseline the time it takes, measure the AI-assisted time, calculate the savings. This is the bucket you lead with when building the business case — because the math is simple and verifiable."

"Quick example: five managers spend three hours a week on reporting. AI cuts it to thirty minutes. That's 12.5 hours per week at $45 per hour — $562 a week, $29,000 a year. From one use case."

**Quality Improvement — measurable with effort:**
"Consistency of output. Reduction in revision cycles. Audit readiness improvements. Reduction in compliance incidents. These are real — they're just harder to put a clean dollar number on."

**Revenue Protection and Growth — highest dollar value, hardest to isolate:**
"No-show reduction, faster credentialing, faster time-to-fill, improved retention. The dollar values here are large. The attribution is more complex. Lead with time savings to build credibility, then layer in revenue protection as the case matures."

### Slide 16 — The Time Savings Calculator

**What to say:**

```
(Time before - Time after) × Hourly rate × Frequency = Annual value

"The question isn't whether the ROI is there. The question is whether you're measuring it."

**Discussion prompt:**

### Slide 18 — The ROI Story Prompt

**What to say:**
"This is the prompt you come back to in 90 days. Once you've been executing the blueprint you're about to build — once you have adoption numbers, time savings data, use cases deployed — this is how you communicate it to leadership in a way they'll actually engage with."

Walk through the prompt. Note the key phrase: "not just data, but narrative."

"Executives don't make sustained investments based on data tables. They make sustained investments based on stories that have data embedded in them. This prompt generates that story."

```

## THE CAPSTONE EXERCISE — FULL INSTRUCTIONS

### Overview

**Exercise:** Build a mock 90-Day AI Readiness Blueprint for Ridgeview Community Health  
**Format:** Table groups of 3–5 participants  
**Time:** 25 minutes total (12 minutes group work, 10 minutes presentations, 3 minutes debrief)  
**Output:** A completed one-page Blueprint per table — presented back to the full room

---

### The Fictional Org Scenario

Read this to the room (or print it on the exercise brief):

---

**Ridgeview Community Health** is a nonprofit community health network with 6 clinic locations and approximately 280 employees across clinical and administrative roles. The organization serves a predominantly underinsured population in a mid-sized metro area.

**Current situation:**
- AI Maturity Level: **Experimentation.** A handful of managers and one clinical director have been using ChatGPT informally for documentation and communications. No formal policy exists.
- **No AI governance policy** is in place. The compliance officer is aware of informal AI use and has raised concerns.
- **Technology footprint:** Microsoft 365 (standard licensing, no Copilot), Epic EHR, standard office productivity tools.
- **Budget reality:** No dedicated AI budget. Any investment needs to come from operational savings or grant funding.
- **Board status:** The board has not been formally briefed on AI strategy. The CEO is supportive but hasn't made it a priority.
- **Known champion candidates:** The Director of Operations (who attended this training) and a high-performing billing supervisor who has been vocal about manual inefficiencies in claims processing.
- **Recent incident:** One front desk coordinator inadvertently entered a patient's name and appointment details into a free consumer AI tool to generate a reminder script. No breach determination was made, but the compliance officer flagged it.

---

### Table Group Task

Each table group will build a **90-Day AI Readiness Blueprint** for Ridgeview. The blueprint has four components:

**1. Top 3 Use Cases to Deploy in the Next 90 Days**
- Identify three specific, high-value use cases for Ridgeview (not generic — specific to their staff roles and pain points)
- For each use case: name the department, describe the task, estimate the time savings using the formula from Slide 16, and identify what tool tier is appropriate

**2. One Governance Action to Take in the Next 30 Days**
- Name the specific action (e.g., draft the acceptable use policy, run a staff AI tool survey, schedule compliance review)
- Identify who owns it
- Identify what success looks like at Day 30

**3. One Champion to Activate**
- Based on the scenario, identify who the best champion candidate is and why
- Describe the specific action to activate them in the next two weeks (not "tell them to share" — a specific, time-bound action)

**4. One Metric to Track**
- Name the one metric that, 90 days from now, would tell Ridgeview's CEO that the initiative is working
- Explain why this metric, and not another one

---

### Setup Instructions

1. Pre-assign table groups before participants arrive. Aim for 3–5 per table. Mix organizations and functions where possible.
2. Print or distribute the exercise brief and the blank Blueprint worksheet (one per table, not one per person — groups build one artifact together).
3. Designate a timekeeper for yourself — not the room. When 10 minutes are up, give a 2-minute warning. When 12 minutes are up, call time.
4. Designate one presenter per table (or let groups self-select) before the timer starts. This prevents the last two minutes being spent deciding who presents.

---

### Debrief Format

Ask **each table** to share their **one champion activation action** and their **one metric**. These two items spark the most productive cross-table discussion and are the most transferable back to real organizations.

After all tables have shared, pose one full-group debrief question:

> "Looking at what every table built — what's the one thing that almost every group included? And what does that tell us about where the biggest opportunity is for organizations at Ridgeview's maturity level?"

Then close the exercise and move to Slide 20.

---

### If a Group Gets Stuck

The most common sticking point is the use case specificity requirement. If a table is listing generic use cases ("use AI for communications"), push them with: "Which staff role is running this prompt? What are they doing right now instead? How long does it take?" Specificity is the exercise.

The second most common sticking point is the metric. Coach them toward one of these if they're spinning: adoption rate (% of staff using AI tools 3× per week), time savings (hours per department per month), or one operational metric they already track (claims denial rate, time-to-fill, patient no-show rate).

## HANDLING COMMON OBJECTIONS

These four objections are specific to Module 4's leadership audience. They are different from the staff-level objections in Modules 1–3.

---

### "We're not ready for governance yet."

**What they mean:** We haven't deployed AI widely enough to justify building the policy infrastructure. Let's do that later.

**What's actually happening:** Governance feels like bureaucracy when adoption is low — but the incidents that create liability happen before adoption is high. The risk is front-loaded; the policy is being pushed to the back.

**What to say:**
> "I hear this often, and here's what I'd push back on: your staff aren't waiting for your governance policy before they use AI. They're using it now, with whatever tools they have access to. The Ridgeview scenario we built today is based on real patterns I see. The question isn't whether your organization is ready for governance — it's whether you'd rather build the policy before or after an incident. Building it before is easier, faster, and costs less."

**Don't argue.** State the risk plainly and move on. Plant the seed.

---

### "Our board isn't bought in."

**What they mean:** I can't move this forward without board approval, and I don't have it.

**What's actually happening:** Either the board hasn't been briefed, or they've been briefed poorly — with hype instead of specifics.

**What to say:**
> "The business case prompt we ran today is your starting point. Boards respond to two things: risk and ROI. The governance section gives you the risk framing — 'here's what's happening now, here's what we're doing about it.' The ROI calculator gives you the opportunity framing — 'here's what we expect to recover.' That's a board presentation. It doesn't require their buy-in on AI philosophy — it requires their buy-in on a specific investment with a specific return. Those are different conversations."

**Optional add:** "If it would be helpful, KEDAL Solutions can support a board briefing. That's a separate engagement, but it's one we've done — it works."

---

### "We don't have the resources to track ROI."

**What they mean:** We're understaffed and can't add a measurement program on top of everything else.

**What's actually happening:** They're picturing a complex analytics infrastructure. They don't need one.

**What to say:**
> "You don't need a BI dashboard to track ROI at the start. You need three things: a list of your top use cases, a stopwatch, and a spreadsheet. Pick one task, baseline it before AI, measure it after. Do that for five tasks across two departments. You now have a business case. The measurement doesn't have to be perfect — it has to be credible. A compliance officer who tracked her time for two weeks and showed a 60% reduction is more persuasive than a theoretical model."

**Key reframe:** Start small. One metric. One department. One quarter. Scale the measurement when you have the story to justify the investment.

---

### "This sounds like more work on top of our existing work."

**What they mean:** I'm already underwater. You're asking me to add an initiative.

**What's actually happening:** They're right. The early stages of AI adoption do require upfront time investment. The question is whether the return justifies it.

**What to say:**
> "You're not wrong. The first 30 days require intentional effort. That's real. Here's what I'd ask you to weigh: what does the work look like in 12 months if you don't build this? Because your competitors — the organizations competing for the same staff, the same contracts, the same patients — some of them are already integrating this into how they operate. The cost of not acting isn't zero. It compounds."

"And practically: the 90-day blueprint you built today is designed to be low-overhead by intent. One use case. One champion. One metric. That's not a program — that's a decision."

**Watch for:** This objection sometimes means "I'm not the right person to own this." If that's what you're sensing, pivot to: "Who in your organization should own this? Because that decision is the first step in the blueprint."

### The 90-Day Check-In — Follow-Up Engagement Opportunity

Module 4 is the natural entry point for a follow-up engagement. The blueprint participants built today has a 90-day horizon. At Day 90, two things are true: (1) they have results to measure and (2) they have new questions they didn't know to ask in the training room.

**Offer a 90-Day Check-In as a structured follow-up:**

> "In 90 days, I want to hear what happened. What you deployed, what the numbers look like, what got stuck. I offer a 90-day check-in for every Module 4 cohort — it's a 60-minute session, just your leadership team, structured around the ROI story prompt we built today. It's the accountability mechanism that turns a training event into an initiative."

Structure of the 90-day check-in session:
| Time | Agenda Item |
|---|---|
| 0–10 min | Each leader shares: what they deployed, what they measured |
| 10–25 min | Run the ROI Story Prompt live with their real data |
| 25–40 min | Where the blueprint stalled and why — problem-solve as a group |
| 40–55 min | Build the next 90-day priorities |
| 55–60 min | Define what success looks like at 6 months |

**Why this works as a business development conversation:**
The 90-day check-in surfaces exactly the needs that a follow-on engagement addresses: deeper workflow redesign, additional staff training cohorts, governance policy finalization, board briefing support. Do not pitch those services in the check-in — let the conversation surface them. Your job in that session is to be useful. The follow-on work will present itself.

**Scheduling:** Plant the seed today. Before the session closes, ask: "Who wants to put a 90-day check-in on the calendar before you leave? I'll send a hold with an agenda." Get 2–3 commitments in the room. This is how the follow-up engagement begins.
