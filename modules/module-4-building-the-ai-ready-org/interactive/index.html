Change management

The Internal Champion Model.

The single most effective driver of AI adoption is peer influence — not mandate, not training.

What makes a champion

✓
Respected by peers — not necessarily the most senior person in the room
✓
Has personally experienced AI value — they've run the prompts and saved the time
✓
Given time and recognition — their champion role is visible, not volunteer extra work
✓
Operates in a specific domain — one department or function, not organization-wide

How to identify them

After every training session, ask participants to rate themselves: "How likely are you to share what you learned today with someone on your team?"

Anyone who rates themselves 4–5 and ran at least one prompt live during the session is a champion candidate. That's your list.

The math: In a room of 20 participants, you typically identify 4–6 champions. Each champion influences 5–8 peers. One training session seeds adoption for 100+ people.

Equip your champions.

Champions don't need to be trainers. They need one good peer session and one good prompt to run it.

The champion peer session prompt

"I've just completed AI skills training and want to share what I learned with my team. Help me design a 20-minute peer learning session. Our team of 8 manages [describe your function]. I want to show them 2–3 use cases from my own work where AI saved me meaningful time. Change management

The 4 objections. The 4 responses.

Every room has these conversations. Know them before they happen.

"AI will take my job."
"AI changes what your job requires — not whether your job exists. The people who learn this become more valuable. The ones who don't get left behind by the people who did. This training is how we make sure that's not you."
"I don't trust the output."
"Good. You shouldn't. Module 2 was entirely about output evaluation. The skill isn't trusting AI — it's knowing when to verify and how. AI is a draft, not a decision."
"This is too complicated."
"You don't need to understand how it works. You need to know how to use it. You use your phone's GPS without understanding satellite triangulation. Same principle."
"We already tried it and it didn't work."
"What specifically didn't work? Most early failures come from the wrong tool, vague prompts, or not knowing how to evaluate output. Those are all solvable. Let's look at what happened."

Governance

HIPAA + AI: the intersection.

The existing HIPAA framework applies to AI use. Four principles your staff must understand.

⚖️

Minimum Necessary

Just because an AI tool can process an entire patient record doesn't mean it should. Use the minimum PHI necessary for the task — same standard as any other PHI disclosure.

📄

Business Associate Agreements

Any AI vendor that processes, creates, or transmits PHI is a Business Associate. BAAs must exist before PHI enters that system. Most common violation: free consumer tools with patient data.

📚

Training Documentation

HIPAA requires documented training for staff who access PHI. AI-specific training — approved tools, permitted data, incident reporting — must be added to HIPAA training records.

🚨

Breach Risk Assessment

The 4-factor breach analysis applies to AI incidents the same as email misdirection. Organizations need a documented process for evaluating AI-related privacy events.

This isn't a 6-month project. It's a 60-day sprint with an ongoing owner.

5
Ongoing

Monitor & Update

Designate an owner. Set a quarterly review date — not annual. AI tools evolve faster than the calendar year.

ROI

Three types of AI return.

AI ROI isn't just time savings. Here's how to see the full picture.

⏱

Time Savings

The most measurable. Identify a task, clock the before, clock the after. Multiply by frequency and headcount.

Example:
5 managers × 3 hrs/wk on reporting → 30 min with AI = 12.5 hrs/week saved = $29K/year from one use case
Difficulty to measure: Low
✅

Quality Improvement

Consistency of output, fewer revision cycles, improved audit readiness, reduction in compliance incidents.

Example:
Job postings reviewed and revised 3× before posting → reviewed once, posted same day. Recruiter time recovered = faster time-to-fill
Difficulty to measure: Medium
💰

Revenue Protection & Growth

No-show reduction, faster credentialing, more proposals submitted, improved retention and reduced turnover cost.

Example:
18% no-show rate → 12% = $200K–$400K revenue recovery for a 12-provider practice
Difficulty to measure: High — but highest dollar value

ROI · Live demo

Build the business case in one session.

The hardest part of sustaining AI investment is translating what you've seen into language a board or CFO will act on.

The business case prompt

"Build an internal business case for investing in AI tools and training for our healthcare organization. We have [X] employees across [departments]. Our top 3 AI use cases and estimated time savings are: [describe]. Structure the business case with: (1) executive summary — problem and opportunity in 2 paragraphs, (2) current state cost analysis with time and dollar estimates per use case, (3) investment required — training, tools, implementation time, (4) projected ROI at 12 and 24 months, (5) risk analysis with mitigations, (6) recommendation and specific ask. Format for C-suite or board presentation."
What this changes: AI adoption stops being a line item that gets cut and becomes a financial decision with quantified returns. The organizations that measure ROI keep investing. The ones that don't treat it as a cost and cut it when budgets tighten.
