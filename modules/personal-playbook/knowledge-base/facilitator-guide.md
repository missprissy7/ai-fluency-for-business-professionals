## HOW TO USE THIS GUIDE

This guide is for Teacher Scott's eyes only. It maps directly to the 22-slide *Personal Playbook* module and is designed to stay open on a second screen while you present.

**This module is intentionally different from the business modules — and so is the facilitation.**

M1–M4 are about organizations. This one is about people. The room you're in might be a corporate conference room, but it might just as easily be a community center, a library meeting room, a church fellowship hall, or a living room. The audience isn't a workforce. It's people — with mortgages, job searches, hard conversations pending, doctors they didn't fully understand, leases they signed without reading.

That changes everything about how you show up.

**What's different about facilitating this module:**

- **Talk in first person.** Share your own examples. "I used this when I was navigating my career transition" lands a hundred times better than "some people find this useful for career changes." The smart-friend voice only works if the smart friend is actually in the room.
- **Less explaining, more showing.** Every use case has a real prompt. Run it live. Let them see it work in real time. The "aha" moment comes from watching it happen, not from hearing you describe it.
- **Lower the stakes.** Some people in this room have never used an AI tool. Some feel embarrassed about that. Make it completely safe to not know things. The whole point of the "smart friend" frame is that your friend doesn't judge you for not knowing.
- **Slow down on personal moments.** When the room gets quiet — when someone nods hard or looks up from their phone — something landed. Give it a beat. Ask "does that sound familiar?" Don't rush past it.
- **Match energy to the section.** Part 1 (Productivity) is energizing and practical. Part 2 (Career) gets a little more personal. Part 3 (Life/Decisions) requires a gentler, more trusted tone. Adjust as you move through the module.

The business modules end with a 30-day plan for the organization. This one ends with participants walking out knowing what *they* are going to try tonight. That's the measure of success.

- Leave your corporate trainer hat at the door. Today you are Kechia first, Teacher Scott second.

## HANDS-ON DELIVERY SETUP

**This is the most important section of this guide. Read it carefully before every session.**

### Why This Module Should Always Be Hands-On

Every other module in the KEDAL curriculum is something you can absorb and take back to your organization. This one is different. The whole point of the Personal Playbook is that participants build a personal AI habit they'll actually continue using after they leave the room. You can't build a habit by watching someone else demonstrate one.

The goal: every participant should leave having run at least one real prompt using their own real-life information — not a fictional scenario, not "imagine you need to write a resume," but *their* resume, *their* situation, *their* week. That moment of "it actually worked for my thing" is what converts a passive listener into an active user.

---

### Three Delivery Options

#### Option A — RECOMMENDED: Everyone on Their Own Device

Every participant signs up for or logs into their own free ChatGPT or Perplexity account at the start of the session. Account setup is not overhead — it *is* part of the experience. Getting someone into a tool for the first time is a victory.

**Best for:** Workshop settings, community sessions, small groups where you have control of the room and at least 5 minutes to spare at the start. Works best when you know ahead of time that participants are bringing devices.

**How to facilitate this:**
- Announce before the session (in the invite, on a sign at the door): *"Bring your phone or laptop — we're going to actually use AI today, not just talk about it."*
- Plan the account setup walkthrough as a formal 5-minute block (see below — it's worth it)
- During each use case, give participants 3–5 minutes to try the prompt themselves with their own information before you move on

#### Option B — Teacher Scott Demos, Then They Try

You demo each use case live on the main screen, then participants try it on their own devices.

**Best for:** Mixed-readiness groups where some are comfortable and some aren't yet. The demo gives them something to follow, then they try it themselves.

**How to facilitate this:**
- Do the demo first, debrief with the room ("what do you notice?"), then release them to try on their devices
- Walk the room while they try — this is your most valuable facilitation moment
- Call out a participant who got a great result (with permission) and show it to the group

#### Option C — Demo-Only

You demo all use cases live; participants observe. No device required.

**Best for:** Settings where device access isn't possible (conference room with no WiFi, participants without devices, very large auditorium settings).

**Adaptation:** If you must go demo-only, make the demos count more. Ask participants to call out what they'd put in the prompt for their own situation. Write it on the board. Run the customized version live. Make it collaborative even when it can't be hands-on.

---

### Account Setup Walkthrough (5-Minute Block)

Run this right after Slide 3 (Guardrails), before Part 1 begins. Introduce it like this:

> "Before we get into the actual use cases — we're going to take five minutes to make sure everyone has a tool open and ready. Because I don't want you watching me do this all day. I want you to actually try it."

**Step-by-step to walk the group through:**

1. **Pick a tool:** ChatGPT (free tier) or Perplexity. Recommend ChatGPT for this session — it handles all 15 use cases well. Perplexity is great for research (P3, C3) and you can mention it for those.

2. **Go to chatgpt.com or perplexity.ai**

3. **Click "Sign up"** — use an existing Google account or email. The signup takes about 60 seconds.

4. **Skip any paid upgrade prompts** — free tier is completely sufficient for everything in this session.

5. **Land on the chat screen** — once they see the text box, they're ready.

**What to say during setup:**
> "If you already have an account, great — just log in. If you're creating one right now, that is absolutely the right thing to be doing. You're two minutes away from having a tool that will save you hours every week."

**If someone is struggling:** Pair them with a neighbor who's already in. This is good — it creates peer-to-peer learning in the room.

**If someone says "I don't want to create an account":** Respect it. Tell them to watch along and try it at home — the prompts will be in their follow-up materials. Don't pressure.

---

### Privacy and PHI Guardrail — Teach This Before the Hands-On Section

Before you release participants to try prompts with their real information, give this 60-second briefing:

> "Quick note before you start entering your own information — and this applies every time you use AI tools. Consumer AI tools like ChatGPT free are not the place for truly sensitive information. The rule I use: if losing it would be catastrophic, don't paste it in. Specifically:
>
> — No Social Security numbers
> — No full financial account numbers
> — No medical information combined with your full name and date of birth — you can describe a situation generally without including your full identity
> — No passwords, obviously
>
> For everything in today's session, you can be real and specific without being fully identified. Your resume without your home address. Your financial situation described in ranges. A medical topic without your full name attached. You'll get just as useful an output — and you've protected yourself."

This is not meant to scare the room. It's a matter-of-fact guardrail that also builds trust. Participants who learn this from you will apply it going forward — and it's one of the most genuinely useful things you can teach.

---

### The Hands-On Moment — What to Actually Do

For each use case, the hands-on moment follows this structure:

1. **Show the slide** — explain the pain point (1 minute)
2. **Run the demo prompt** live on your screen (1–2 minutes)
3. **Debrief** — "what do you notice? what would you change?" (30 seconds)
4. **Release for participant practice** — "Now try it with your own situation. You have three minutes." (Option A/B only)
5. **Call out a result** — ask if anyone wants to share what they got (30 seconds)

The key instruction for participants during their practice time:

> "Don't use a fake example. Use your real week. Your real resume. Your actual situation. The prompt will be twice as useful when the context is real."

### Slide 1 — Cover

**Before you click Begin:**

Don't open with the slides. Open with a question. Let the room settle, look around, and ask:

> "Think about the last time you had something on your plate that you just kept putting off — not because it was hard, but because it felt like it would take forever to start. Something you needed to research, or write, or figure out, or have a conversation about. Does anyone have something like that right now?"

Let two or three people answer. Keep it light — you're not asking them to share trauma, just to locate themselves in their current life. You want the room to arrive present in their personal context, not their professional one.

Then: "Everything we do today is for that list. By the time you leave, at least one thing on it is going to be a lot easier."

Click Begin.

**Personal story prompt for Teacher Scott:**
Share one thing you personally had on your plate when you first started using AI for your life — not for KEDAL, not for curriculum design, but for your own life. A decision you were avoiding. A conversation you were dreading. Something you finally got done.

> *Example frame: "When I was going through my own career transition, I had this massive mental load of things I needed to figure out — and I kept telling myself I didn't have time. When I actually started using AI as my thinking partner for personal stuff, not just work stuff, that backlog started clearing. That's what today is about."*

---

### Slide 2 — One Tool. Every Part of Your Life.

**What to say:**

> "The thing I want you to walk away with today is not a list of AI features. It's a shift in how you think about this tool. Right now, most people either don't use AI at all, or they use it for one narrow thing — maybe they've generated an email or two, maybe they've Googled something through it. What we're doing today is expanding that picture."

> "One tool. Used well. Across every part of your actual life. That's the playbook."

Walk through the three categories — Time, Career, Life — and frame each briefly. Don't go deep yet. This is a menu, not the meal.

**Watch for:** Skepticism at this slide. Someone will think "that sounds like a lot." Acknowledge it preemptively:
> "I know that sounds like a big promise. I don't expect you to use AI for all of this by next week. But I do want you to leave today having tried at least one thing that surprises you with how well it works. One thing. That's enough to start."

---

### Slide 3 — The Three Guardrails

**What to say:**

> "Before we start, three rules. Not company-policy rules — more like the rules a friend who's been doing this for a while would give you."

Walk through each one:

**Guardrail 1 — AI drafts, you decide:**
> "AI is not the authority. It's the starting point. Especially for anything medical, legal, or financial — you use AI to get informed and get prepared, and then you bring that to a real professional. This is not about replacing experts. It's about being a better-informed client."

**Guardrail 2 — Specific beats vague:**
> "The more specific you are, the better the output. 'Help me write an email' gets you a generic email. 'Write a polite but firm response to my landlord who hasn't returned my security deposit after 45 days, referencing that my state law requires return within 30 days' gets you something you can actually send. The context is the magic."

**Guardrail 3 — Verify anything important:**
> "Numbers. Dates. Legal deadlines. Medical specifics. Always verify. AI can be wrong about specifics. It's excellent at drafting, reasoning, and structuring — it's less reliable as a source of truth for high-stakes facts. Check the things that matter."

**[HANDS-ON] — Account Setup Block:**
After guardrails, if using Option A or B, run the 5-minute account setup walkthrough now (see Hands-On Delivery Setup section above).

### Slide 5 — P1: The Weekly Brain Dump

**The pain point:** Articulate it fully before showing the solution. Say:

> "Let me describe a feeling. It's Sunday at 7pm. You have things due this week. You have things you promised people. You have things only you know need to happen. They live in your head, in three different apps, in a text you sent yourself, in a calendar notification you snoozed four times. The week hasn't started and you're already behind. Show of hands — is that a real feeling?"

Wait for hands. Then:

> "Here's what AI does with that. You dump everything. Everything — messy, incomplete, 'I also need to call my mom.' You dump it into the tool and it gives you back a structured week."

**[DEMO] — Run the Brain Dump prompt live:**

```
Here's everything on my plate this week — it's messy, but here's the brain dump: [paste a real, messy list of your own tasks for the week]. Help me turn this into a structured weekly plan. Group tasks by type (urgent, important-not-urgent, delegatable). Suggest which days to tackle which clusters. Flag anything that's likely to take longer than I think. Give me one clear "start here" task for Monday morning.
```

Use your *actual* task list for the week when possible. If this is a live session, paste in the real Teacher Scott to-do list. The authenticity lands.

**[HANDS-ON]:** "Your turn. Pull out your phone, open ChatGPT or whatever note-taking app has your to-do list, and spend 3 minutes trying this with your real week. Don't clean it up first. Messy is better."

**Personal story prompt for Teacher Scott:** Share a specific time the brain dump changed your week. What was on the list? What did the output look like? What happened on Monday morning?

**Watch for:** Someone who says "I don't have that many tasks." Redirect: "Even five things is worth doing this with — the value isn't the quantity, it's the structure and the clear starting point."

### Slide 6 — P2: Email That Writes Itself

**What to say:**

> "Email is the single most universally annoying communication task in adult life. And I don't just mean work email. I mean the email to the contractor who disappeared. The email to the school about your kid's situation. The thank-you email you've been meaning to write for three weeks. The response to a difficult family member."

> "Most of us spend 20 minutes on an email that takes 20 seconds to say out loud. AI fixes this."

**[DEMO] — Run the Email prompt live:**

```
Write an email for me. Context: My contractor completed work on my bathroom in January and I have a punch list of three unfinished items he promised to fix by February 15th. It's now been six weeks with no response to my last two texts. Recipient: My contractor — professional relationship, but I'm a paying customer and I'm frustrated. What I need to communicate: I expect the items completed within 10 business days or I'll be leaving a review and contacting my state contractor licensing board. Tone: Firm but professional — not aggressive, but he needs to know I'm serious. Keep it concise.
```

Let the room react to the output before you say anything about it.

**Personal story prompt for Teacher Scott:** The email you didn't want to write — one specific example. A landlord, a difficult work situation, a family boundary. What was the output like? Did you send it as-is or edit it? What happened?

**Watch for:** "That email sounds too formal for how I actually talk." Perfect opportunity to show the follow-up prompt: "Make it sound more like me — less formal, but still firm." Run it live. This demonstrates iteration, which is half the skill.

### Slide 7 — P3: Research Without the Rabbit Hole

**What to say:**

> "I want to ask a question. How many of you have gone online to research something — a medical question, a product, a neighborhood — and come out the other side more confused than you went in? That's not a you problem. That's how the internet is designed."

> "AI flips this. You tell it exactly what you want to know, and it synthesizes an answer to your actual question. No 47 tabs. No conflicting articles from 2015. Just: here's what you need to know."

**[DEMO] — Use Perplexity for this one, or ChatGPT — pick a live research question:**

```
I need to research [a relevant current topic — pick something real and relatable: a common appliance, a neighborhood decision, a health topic in the news]. My specific question is: [your actual question]. Give me: (1) the key facts I need to understand this topic, (2) the 2–3 things most people get wrong or miss, (3) what I should do or decide next based on this information. Keep it practical, not encyclopedic.
```

**[HANDS-ON]:** "What's something you've been meaning to look up but keep avoiding the rabbit hole? Try this prompt with that exact question right now."

**Watch for:** Someone who asks about accuracy. This is a great lead-in to Guardrail 3 (verify what matters) — don't be defensive, lean into it: "Exactly right. For research, Perplexity is especially good because it cites its sources. For a cooking question — you don't need to verify. For a medical symptom — check with your doctor. The tool for the task, and judgment for the output."

### Slide 8 — P4: Prepare for the Hard Conversation

**Tone shift here. Slow down. This one lands differently.**

**What to say:**

> "This is the use case I'm most personally invested in. Not because it's the most efficient one — but because of what it costs people when they don't have it."

> "Most people carry conversations they're dreading for days. Weeks sometimes. Lying awake the night before, running through scenarios. What if they say this? What if I freeze? What if I say the wrong thing? And most of the time, we go in underprepared because we don't have anyone to think through it with."

> "AI can be that thinking partner. It helps you figure out what you actually want to say, anticipates what they might say back, and lets you practice before the real conversation."

**[DEMO] — Hard Conversation prep prompt:**

```
I have a difficult conversation coming up and I want to prepare. The situation: I need to tell my mother that I can't take on the responsibility she's been expecting me to take regarding my aunt's care — I've been the default family coordinator for two years and it's affecting my health and my own family. The person: My mother — she means well but tends to get emotional and make me feel guilty. My relationship with her is close but this is a topic we've never directly addressed. What I want to accomplish: Set a clear boundary without destroying the relationship. Help me: (1) draft what I want to open with, (2) anticipate the 3 most likely pushback responses and how I'd answer each, (3) identify the one thing I should avoid saying. Then offer to role-play the conversation with me.
```

This demo is meant to feel real. You don't have to use this exact scenario — use a real one from your life if you're comfortable (see personal story prompt below).

After the demo, pause. Then: "Did that feel familiar to anyone?"

**[HANDS-ON]:** "Think of a conversation you've been putting off. It doesn't have to be heavy. Could be a conversation with a coworker, a negotiation with a landlord, a family planning discussion. Try the prompt with whatever's actually on your mind. You don't have to share it with anyone."

**Personal story prompt for Teacher Scott:** This is your most important personal story moment in the session. Share a real conversation you prepped for with AI. Ideally something that surprised you — either by how well the prep worked, or by something the AI surfaced that you hadn't considered.

> *Example frame: "I've used this for a conversation about a family responsibility that I'd been avoiding for a long time. Writing out what I wanted to accomplish before the conversation changed how I walked into it. I wasn't just hoping it would go okay — I had a structure. That made a real difference."*

**Watch for:** The room getting quiet and reflective. Let that happen. Don't rush to the next slide. The hard conversation slide earns its time.

### Slide 9 — P5: Summarize Anything

**What to say:**

> "Last one in Part 1, and this one is simple — but don't underestimate it. How many of you have something sitting in your inbox or on your desk that you technically need to read but haven't gotten to? A lease. A policy document. A long email thread. A report someone sent you two weeks ago."

> "You can paste almost any document into AI and ask for a plain-language summary. The five things I need to know. The action items. The red flags. Here's how."

**[DEMO] — Quick, punchy demo:**

```
Summarize the following document for me. I want: (1) the key points in plain language, (2) anything that requires my attention or decision, (3) any red flags or things that seem unusual. [Paste a section of a real document — a terms of service, a lease clause, a long email]
```

**[HANDS-ON]:** "If you have a long email on your phone right now, paste it in. Or try the terms and conditions from an app you use. See what comes back."

**Watch for:** People trying to paste PDFs. Quick note: some AI tools handle PDFs directly (ChatGPT with file upload), some don't. If they're copying text, it works fine. If they're uploading a file, tell them to use ChatGPT with the file upload feature.

### Slide 11 — C1: Resume That Gets Read

**What to say:**

> "Your resume is probably fine. But fine doesn't get callbacks. And the people who are getting callbacks right now — in every industry, at every level — they have resumes that are tailored to specific jobs, written in the language of that specific job description, with quantified accomplishments wherever possible. Most people don't have that. Most people are sending the same document everywhere and wondering why they're not hearing back."

> "AI closes this gap in about 10 minutes."

**[DEMO] — Resume tailoring prompt:**

```
Here is my current resume: [paste resume — use a generalized version]. Here is the job description I'm applying for: [paste JD]. Rewrite my resume to: (1) match the language and keywords in this job description, (2) quantify my accomplishments wherever possible — ask me for numbers if you need them, (3) lead with my strongest relevant experience for this specific role. Keep it to one page if possible.
```

**[HANDS-ON]:** "If you have your resume on your phone or laptop — this is the moment to actually try this. Pull up a job you're interested in — it doesn't have to be one you're actively applying for. Paste both in. See what comes back."

**Personal story prompt for Teacher Scott:** Share your own experience with using AI for career positioning — either for your KEDAL work, your own bio, or a past job search. What changed when you started treating AI as a career tool?

**Watch for:** "My resume is fine, I'm not looking for a job right now." Pivot: "This works just as well for updating your LinkedIn summary, writing a professional bio, or preparing for a performance review. Same skill, different document."

### Slide 12 — C2: Interview Prep That Actually Works

**What to say:**

> "Let me tell you what bad interview prep looks like. Reading a list of 'common interview questions' and telling yourself you'll think about them in the parking lot. Then freezing when they ask you a behavioral question and you give a vague, rambling answer that doesn't actually show what you did."

> "Let me tell you what good interview prep looks like. You give AI the job, your background, and your actual experience. It builds you a complete prep kit and then conducts a mock interview with you — right there, in the chat. You can try your answer, get feedback, try again. By the time you walk in, you've said the words out loud twenty times."

**[DEMO] — Interview prep prompt:**

```
I have an interview for a [program manager role] at a [nonprofit healthcare organization]. My background is [10 years in operations, pivoting from healthcare staffing to program management — include relevant experience]. Help me prepare: (1) craft a strong 90-second "tell me about yourself" answer tailored to this role, (2) generate the 8 most likely interview questions for this type of role, (3) help me build a STAR-format answer for 3 behavioral questions using my actual experience. Then offer to conduct a mock interview with me.
```

After showing the output: "And then, when you say 'let's do the mock interview' — it actually does it. It asks you questions one at a time and gives you feedback."

**[HANDS-ON]:** "If you have an upcoming interview — or even a job you're quietly interested in — try this now. Even if the interview is months away, build the prep kit today while you have the tool open."

### Slide 13 — C3: Negotiating What You're Worth

**Take a second before starting this one. This slide matters more than most people realize.**

**What to say:**

> "I'm going to say something direct. Most people leave money on the table every single time they accept a job offer without negotiating. Every time. The research on this is not ambiguous — people who negotiate get 5 to 15 percent more. Most people don't negotiate because they don't know what the market rate is, they don't know how to ask, and they're afraid of the conversation."

> "AI solves the first two of those three problems completely."

**[DEMO] — Use Perplexity first for market data, then ChatGPT for the script:**

```
I'm preparing to negotiate my salary for a [Senior Project Manager] role in [Atlanta, GA]. My current offer is [$78,000]. I have [8] years of experience and [PMP certification, background in healthcare operations]. Research: (1) the current market range for this role in this area, (2) the top factors that justify the higher end of the range. Then help me write: (3) the exact email or script I'll use to counter, (4) my response if they push back or say the offer is firm.
```

Show the output. Then read the counter-offer email out loud. Let the room feel how concrete it is.

> "You don't have to wing this conversation. You can walk into it with actual data and actual words."

**Personal story prompt for Teacher Scott:** Share a moment when having a negotiation prep — or watching someone else use AI for negotiation prep — changed the outcome. Or share what you would have done differently earlier in your career if you'd had this tool.

**[HANDS-ON]:** "Think about the last time you accepted a number without negotiating. What would you know now that you didn't know then? Try this prompt with that situation — even retroactively. It's good practice for the next time."

**Watch for:** "What if they take back the offer?" Respond: "That almost never happens with a politely worded counter, and when it does, you've learned something important about that employer. The fear of asking is almost always bigger than the actual risk."

## HANDLING COMMON REACTIONS

*Note: In this module, these are not "objections" — they're the natural reactions of real people being asked to try something new in a personal context. Meet them there.*

---

### "I wouldn't even know what to say to it."

**This is the most common reaction from first-timers. Meet it warmly.**

> "That's exactly why we're doing this today. The only way to get comfortable with it is to actually say something to it — anything. Here's the thing: AI is not looking for perfect phrasing. You can be messy. You can say 'I'm not sure how to ask this but...' and it will still give you something useful. The brain dump prompt is the perfect one to start with, because messy is actually *better* for that one. You literally cannot do it wrong."

**If they're still hesitant:** Walk to their device with them. Help them type the first few words. Sometimes all it takes is someone else typing the first word.

---

### "What if it gives me wrong information?"

**Don't minimize this. Validate it first.**

> "That's the right question, and I'm glad you asked it. AI can be wrong — especially about specific facts, current information, and medical or legal specifics. This is exactly why Guardrail 3 exists: verify what matters. Here's how I think about it: AI is very good at drafting, explaining, and structuring. It's less reliable as a source of truth for high-stakes specifics. So for a lease clause — read the clause yourself after AI explains it. For a medical question — bring what you learned to your doctor. For a negotiation number — cross-reference with a site like Glassdoor or the Bureau of Labor Statistics. Use AI as the starting point, not the final word."

---

### "Is this private? I'm not sure I want to put my personal stuff in there."

**This is a reasonable concern. Be honest about it.**

> "Totally reasonable to ask. Here's the honest answer: consumer AI tools like the free version of ChatGPT are not designed for highly sensitive data — think of them the way you'd think of a Google search. You wouldn't type your social security number into Google; don't type it into ChatGPT. But your general life situation? Your resume? A description of a family conversation? That's in the same category as things you'd type into a search engine or write in a notes app. We covered the specific guardrails earlier — no SSNs, no full financial account numbers, no medical info tied to your full identity. Within those guardrails, you're using it the way it's designed to be used."

> "OpenAI and Perplexity both have privacy settings that let you turn off conversation history, which means your prompts aren't used for training. I'd show you where that setting is — it takes 30 seconds to turn on and might make you feel better."

**Follow-through:** Show where the privacy setting is in ChatGPT (Settings → Data Controls → Improve the model for everyone → Off). Worth the 60 seconds.

---

### "I'm not a tech person."

**This is not about tech. Say that clearly.**

> "You don't need to be. I want to be really clear about that. You don't need to understand how it works — you need to know how to talk to it. That's it. And you've been talking to people your whole life. AI is just a very fast, very patient thinking partner that responds in text. If you can write a text message, you can use AI. The prompts I've been showing you today — they're all just writing a message explaining what you need. That's the whole skill."

**Follow-through:** Hand them the simplest prompt in the deck — the email prompt or the summarize prompt — and walk them through typing it themselves. First-time success matters.

---

### "I already use it a little, but only for work things."

**This person is your ally. Elevate them.**

> "That's a perfect place to be coming from — you already know it works. What we're doing today is just expanding where you use it. Everything you've been doing for work, you can do for your own life. The resume, the hard conversation, the health question you've been meaning to research — same tool, same skill, different context. The people who get the most out of AI are the ones who stop thinking of it as a work tool and start thinking of it as a life tool."

**Invite them to share:** "What's the most useful thing you've used it for at work? Let's see if we can apply that same approach to something personal."
