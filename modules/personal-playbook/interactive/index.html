Before we start

Three things to know.

This isn't magic. It's a very capable tool with a few rules that make it useful instead of frustrating.

1

AI drafts. You decide.

Especially for health, legal, and financial matters — AI gives you a well-organized starting point. Your judgment, and when needed a professional, makes the final call. This is true for every tool in this playbook.

2

Specific beats vague. Always.

The more context you give it, the better the output. "Write me an email" gets you something generic. "Write me an email to my landlord explaining that the heat has been broken for 3 weeks and I need a repair date in writing" gets you something you can actually send.

3

Verify anything important.

Numbers, dates, medical specifics, legal requirements — double-check them. AI is extremely good at structure, tone, and reasoning. It occasionally makes up facts. For anything high-stakes, confirm the specifics before you act on them.

Your Time

Five ways to get back hours every week — starting today.

P · 01

Your Time

The Weekly Brain Dump

Sound familiar? Sunday anxiety — the week ahead feels unmanageable. Tasks are scattered across your head, your phone, three apps, and a sticky note.

The prompt

"Here's everything on my plate this week — it's messy, but here's the brain dump: [paste everything]. Help me turn this into a structured weekly plan. Group tasks by type: urgent, important-not-urgent, and delegatable. Suggest which days to tackle which clusters. Flag anything that's likely to take longer than I think. Give me one clear 'start here' task for Monday morning."

What happens

  • Your chaos becomes a structured week plan
  • Priorities are surfaced, not buried
  • Monday has a clear starting point
Before: Sunday anxiety, scattered lists → After: 3-minute plan, clear week
Try this now: Open any AI tool and paste everything on your mind right now. You don't need to clean it up first. The mess is the input.
P · 02

Your Time

Email That Writes Itself

Sound familiar? Staring at a blank reply for 20 minutes. The email isn't complicated — but the tone, the phrasing, the "how do I say this without it coming out wrong" drains more energy than the task deserves.

The prompt

"Write an email for me. Context: [explain the situation]. Recipient: [who they are and your relationship]. What I need to say: [key points]. Tone: [polite but firm / warm and personal / professional but friendly]. Keep it concise — I hate long emails."
Then try: "Make it shorter" · "Make it warmer" · "Add that I'm available by phone"

Works for

  • Difficult replies to a landlord, employer, or contractor
  • Thank-you notes that actually sound genuine
  • Saying no without damaging a relationship
  • Following up without sounding desperate
  • Cold outreach that doesn't read as spam
Before: 15–20 min of staring and rewrites → After: Draft in 30 sec, sent in 3 min
P · 03

Your Time

Research Without the Rabbit Hole

Sound familiar? You need to research something — a symptom, a neighborhood, a car repair, a new appliance. 90 minutes later you're 15 tabs deep and more confused than when you started.

The prompt

"I need to research [topic]. My specific question is: [what you actually want to know]. Give me: (1) the key facts I need to understand this topic, (2) the 2–3 things most people get wrong or miss, (3) what I should do or decide next based on this. Keep it practical, not encyclopedic."
Best tool: Perplexity — gives you sources you can actually check

What you get

  • Your actual question answered — not 47 search results
  • The things you didn't know to ask about
  • A clear next step, not more reading
The shift: You stop being a researcher and start being a decision-maker. AI does the synthesis; you do the judgment.
P · 04

Your Time

Prepare for the Hard Conversation

Sound familiar? You have a conversation you've been dreading — asking for a raise, talking to a difficult family member, confronting a friend. You spend days anxious about it instead of ready for it.

The prompt

"I have a difficult conversation coming up. The situation: [explain]. The person: [who they are, your relationship, their likely reaction]. What I want to accomplish: [your goal]. Help me: (1) draft what I want to open with, (2) anticipate the 3 most likely pushback responses and how I'd answer each, (3) identify the one thing I should avoid saying. Then offer to role-play the conversation with me."

What you get

You walk into the conversation prepared, not just worried. You've already heard the hardest pushback responses — in a safe space — so nothing in the real conversation surprises you.

  • Opening line you've already practiced
  • Responses to the 3 things you're most afraid they'll say
  • A clear goal you can hold onto if it gets emotional
P · 05

Your Time

Summarize Anything

Sound familiar? Long email threads, terms and conditions, insurance documents, articles you were supposed to read — things you technically need to understand but don't have time to read carefully.

The prompt

"Summarize this for me. I want: (1) the key points in plain language, (2) anything that requires my attention or a decision from me, (3) any red flags or anything that seems unusual. [Paste the document, article, or thread]"

Works for

  • Long email threads — "what do I actually need to respond to?"
  • Terms & conditions before signing anything
  • News articles, academic papers, research
  • Meeting notes or transcripts
  • Insurance policies and financial documents
Before: 20-min careful read → After: 45-second summary + action items

C · 01

Your Career

Resume That Gets Read

The problem: Your resume is fine. But "fine" doesn't get callbacks. It needs to speak the exact language of each job posting — and most people send the same resume everywhere.

The prompt

"Here is my current resume: [paste]. Here is the job description I'm applying for: [paste JD]. Rewrite my resume to: (1) match the language and keywords in this job description, (2) quantify my accomplishments — ask me for numbers if you need them, (3) lead with my strongest relevant experience for this specific role. One page if possible."

What changes

  • Keywords match what the ATS system is scanning for
  • Your experience is framed for this specific role
  • Accomplishments are quantified — not just described
Before: 2–3 hrs tailoring per application → After: 10 min, tailored every time
The math: Tailored resumes get 40% more callbacks than generic ones. Most people don't tailor because it takes too long. Now it doesn't.
C · 02

Your Career

Interview Prep That Actually Works

The problem: "Tell me about yourself" — and you freeze, ramble, or answer the question they asked instead of the one they meant.

The prompt

"I have an interview for [role] at [type of company]. My background is [brief summary]. Help me: (1) craft a 90-second 'tell me about yourself' tailored to this role, (2) generate the 8 most likely interview questions for this type of role, (3) help me build a STAR-format answer for 3 behavioral questions using my actual experience. Then offer to conduct a mock interview with me."

The mock interview

After you build your answers, tell the AI: "Now interview me. Ask me your questions one at a time and give me feedback on each answer."

You'll do the actual interview having already practiced the hardest questions — with feedback — in a space where it didn't count.

Tools: ChatGPT GPT-4o Claude
C · 03

Your Career

Negotiate What You're Worth

The problem: Most people don't negotiate. Or they do it without data, without a script, and fold the moment there's any pushback.

The prompt

"I'm preparing to negotiate my salary for [role] in [city]. Current offer: [amount]. My experience: [X years, key skills]. Research: (1) market range for this role in this area, (2) factors that justify the high end. Then write: (3) the exact counter email or script I'll use, (4) my response if they say the offer is firm."
Start with: Perplexity — for current market salary data with sources

of employers have room to negotiate beyond their first offer

5–15%

typical salary increase from negotiating — on the same job

~60%

of people never negotiate at all — usually because they don't know how

C · 04

Your Career

Build Your Personal Brand

The problem: You know you should be more visible professionally — LinkedIn, a clear bio, a point of view — but writing about yourself feels awkward and everything you draft sounds either generic or braggy.

The prompt

"Help me build my professional brand. Who I am: [role, industry, experience]. What I'm most passionate about professionally: [interests/values]. What I want to be known for: [your goal]. Give me: (1) a LinkedIn headline + summary, (2) 3 LinkedIn post ideas with different angles on topics I care about, (3) a 3-sentence professional bio I can use anywhere."

What this unlocks

  • A LinkedIn profile that actually reflects who you are now
  • Content ideas you can post consistently — not blank page panic
  • A bio that opens doors in introductions, proposals, and pitches
Key note: Tell AI your actual voice — "I'm direct and a little sarcastic" or "I'm warm and care deeply about community." The output will sound like you, not like a LinkedIn template.
C · 05

Your Career

Learn Anything Faster

The problem: You want to learn something new — a skill, a software, a subject — but formal courses are too slow, too expensive, or not quite right for what you actually need.

The prompt

"I want to learn [topic/skill]. My current level: [beginner / basics / intermediate]. My goal: [what you want to be able to do]. Time available: [X hrs/week]. Design a learning plan: what to learn first, in what order, what resources to use, and how to practice. Then teach me the first concept right now."

AI as your tutor

You have a personal tutor available 24/7 that explains things at your exact level, in your preferred style, with as many examples and practice questions as you need.

Unlike a course, it answers your specific questions — not the questions the curriculum assumes you have.

The habit: After any lesson, ask: "Give me 3 practice questions on what we just covered." That's the difference between passive learning and retention.

L · 01

Your Life

Understand Any Legal Document

The problem: Lease agreements, contracts, terms and conditions, insurance policies — documents you're supposed to read, don't have time to fully understand, and sign anyway.

The prompt

"Explain this document to me in plain language. I need to know: (1) what I'm agreeing to in simple terms, (2) any obligations that could cost me money, time, or rights, (3) anything that seems unusual for a standard [lease / contract / policy], (4) the 3 most important things to know before I sign. [Paste document]"

Important

⚖️

AI gives you a plain-language summary — not legal advice. If something looks risky or the stakes are high, talk to a lawyer. But you'll walk into that conversation knowing exactly what to ask — which saves attorney time and your money.

  • Lease renewal terms decoded before you sign
  • Employment contract clauses flagged
  • App terms you actually need to know about
  • Insurance policy exclusions surfaced
L · 02

Your Life

Health Research You Can Actually Use

The problem: You leave a doctor's appointment with more questions than when you arrived. Or you go online and scare yourself with worst-case scenarios that may not even apply to your situation.

The prompt

"Help me understand [diagnosis / medication / test result / procedure]. Explain: (1) what this means in plain language, (2) the most common treatment approaches and what the evidence says, (3) questions I should ask my doctor at my next appointment, (4) things I should watch for or tell my doctor right away. Clear language — I'm not a medical professional."

The real value

🩺

AI doesn't replace your doctor. It helps you have a better conversation with them. You walk in informed, with specific questions, and you understand the answers instead of nodding along.

Before every appointment: "I have an appointment with my doctor about [issue]. What questions should I ask to make sure I understand my situation and my options fully?"
L · 03

Your Life

Navigate Home Buying & Renting

The problem: Buying or renting a home involves more unfamiliar terms, hidden costs, and potential mistakes than almost any other transaction in adult life. Most people figure it out by making expensive errors.

The prompt

"I'm [buying / renting] a home and I have questions about [the inspection process / what's in my lease / what closing costs I can negotiate / what to ask the landlord]. Give me: (1) a plain-language explanation of how this works, (2) the 5 things most people miss or get wrong, (3) specific questions to ask [the realtor / landlord / inspector / lender] before moving forward."

Use throughout the process

  • "Explain what an earnest money deposit is and when I could lose it"
  • "What are red flags in a home inspection report I shouldn't ignore?"
  • "Which closing costs are negotiable and how do I ask?"
  • "What should I look for in a lease renewal that benefits the landlord?"
  • "Explain the difference between pre-qualification and pre-approval"
L · 04

Your Life

Personal Finance — Understand and Plan

The problem: Financial planning feels overwhelming, professional advice is expensive, and most people avoid it until something goes wrong.

The prompt

"Help me think through my personal finances. My situation: [income range, main expenses, any debt, savings goal]. I want to understand: (1) how to prioritize between [paying down debt / emergency fund / retirement / a specific goal], (2) what I should know about [401k / Roth IRA / credit score / insurance], (3) a practical action plan for the next 30 days that doesn't require a financial advisor."

What AI does here

💰

AI gives you a framework. A certified financial planner gives you personalized advice. AI makes that planner conversation more productive — and you'll know when you actually need one versus when you can act on your own.

Start here: "Explain the difference between a Roth IRA and a traditional IRA in plain language, and tell me which situations make each one better."
L · 05

Your Life

Family & Hard Conversations

The problem: Parenting, caregiving, and family relationships throw questions at you constantly — how to talk to a child about something hard, how to navigate a conflict, how to handle a situation you've never faced before.

The prompt

"I need help navigating a family situation. [Describe: who's involved, what happened, what you're trying to accomplish]. Help me: (1) understand this from the other person's perspective — what might they be feeling or experiencing, (2) figure out the right approach for this situation, (3) give me specific language I can use — how to start, what to say if it goes sideways. Tone: practical and warm, not clinical."

Real examples

  • "How do I talk to my 10-year-old about [difficult topic] in language they can actually understand?"
  • "Help me set a limit with a family member without it turning into a fight"
  • "I'm helping care for an aging parent — what questions should I be asking their doctor?"
  • "Help me write a message to [person] that addresses what happened without making it worse"
